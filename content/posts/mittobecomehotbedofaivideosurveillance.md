MIT is investing over $3 million in a new AI video surveillance system, comprising over 500 cameras to be installed in academic buildings, residence halls, and outdoor areas. The installation, which began in November 2025, is expected to be completed by September 2026. The cameras will be equipped with advanced features such as real-time face and object classification, motion detection, and the ability to classify individuals based on clothing color, gender, and age.

The cameras, part of Hanwha's Wisenet AI line, will utilize deep learning algorithms to identify and classify multiple objects, supporting resolutions ranging from 2MP to 4K. They will also be able to recognize faces, license plates, and vehicles in real-time. The cameras will be monitored continually using Ai-RGUS, an AI camera software, and will have a wide range of motion capabilities, including pan, tilt, rotate, and zoom. The collected data will be retained for up to 30 days, unless an exception is granted.

The implementation of the surveillance system has raised questions about its effectiveness in preventing crimes and shootings. Critics argue that cameras can only record and warn after the fact, rather than preventing incidents from occurring. The decision to invest in the surveillance system has been justified by MIT as a response to security concerns, but some are skeptical about the true motives and potential consequences of such a widespread surveillance system.
