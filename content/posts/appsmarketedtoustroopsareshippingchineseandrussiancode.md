A recent study examined hundreds of mobile apps marketed to US military personnel and found that over one in eight contained software built by companies in China, Russia, or other foreign nations. This raises concerns that adversary governments could harvest data revealing sensitive information about service members, including their locations, deployments, and routines. The study found that 64% of the apps contained third-party code, known as SDKs, which can track user behavior and share information with outside companies.

The researchers discovered that 12 apps contained code from Huawei, a Chinese telecom company flagged as a national security threat by US regulators in 2020. Additionally, two apps were built by Russian companies and incorporated the Russian ad service Yandex. The study also found that 40% of the apps collected or shared more data than they disclosed in their store listings. The most common SDKs came from Google and Facebook, but code was also traced back to other countries, including Israel, India, and Germany.

The study's findings have significant implications for the security and privacy of US military personnel. Experts warn that the data harvested from these apps could aid foreign spies in identifying personnel with access to sensitive sites, mapping facility guard schedules, and surfacing other compromising details. The researchers surveyed military-affiliated Americans and found that over 83% used at least one app that made them uncomfortable, and 76-83% were extremely uncomfortable with apps containing code from China, Russia, Iran, or North Korea. The study's authors recommend in-phone warnings, federal laws restricting data brokers, and stricter bans on foreign code in military-marketed apps to mitigate these risks.
