The topic of paying hackers to prevent data leaks has become a hot issue. Recently, companies like Grafana have taken a "no pay" approach, while others have made "agreements" with hackers, which is a euphemism for paying ransom. This normalization of ransom payments is a concern, as it may encourage more hackers to engage in such activities.

The language used by companies to describe these situations can be misleading, downplaying the severity of the crime. For example, Instructure stated that they "reached an agreement with the unauthorized actor involved", which waters down the fact that they paid extortionists. This kind of language can contribute to the normalization of ransom payments and obscure the criminal nature of the act.

The current trend seems to be that "pay or leak" is becoming the new norm, with companies either paying hackers or risking their data being leaked. Some statements, such as "the data was returned to us", can be misleading and do not address the underlying issue. The situation highlights the need for a more transparent and honest approach to dealing with cyber threats and ransom demands.
