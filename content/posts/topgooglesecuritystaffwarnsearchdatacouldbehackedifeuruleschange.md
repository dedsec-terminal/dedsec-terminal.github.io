Here is a 3-paragraph summary of the article:

Google's top security staff have warned that proposed EU rules to open up its search data and Android operating system to competitors could lead to people's search queries being hacked and an increase in cybercrime. The rules, part of the European Union's Digital Markets Act, aim to reduce Big Tech companies' dominance and make it easier for others to compete. However, Google's vice president of security engineering, Heather Adkins, claims that the proposed changes could result in a significant increase in fraud and cybercrime, with bad actors potentially de-anonymizing search queries and targeting small companies that receive shared data.

The EU's proposals require Google to share anonymized search data with rivals, including query input and metadata, as well as click data and ranking results. However, Google argues that the proposed anonymization techniques are flawed and could be easily reidentified by bad actors. The company claims that its security team has proven that the data can be reidentified in under two hours, and that the plans could put millions of people's private search queries at risk. Google is calling for a "middle ground" to be found, with Adkins suggesting that large language models could be used to help anonymize data and prevent it from being exploited by hackers.

The EU's proposals have sparked a divisive debate among privacy advocates, academics, and lawyers, with some arguing that the plans do not go far enough to protect user privacy, while others believe that Google's concerns are unfounded. Some competitors, such as DuckDuckGo, argue that the risks of reidentification can be mitigated within the existing framework, while others, such as Brave, believe that the proposals create a "severe privacy risk". The European Commission is set to make a final decision on the proposals by July 27, and Google is urging caution and a more nuanced approach to ensure that user data is protected.
