A critical surveillance authority, Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, is set to expire on June 12, with no clear path to a deal. The authority allows the government to collect foreign targets' communications without a warrant, but also sweeps in Americans' messages. President Donald Trump has refused to abandon his pick of Bill Pulte, a housing official with no intelligence background, to temporarily lead the US intelligence community, which has become a major sticking point in the reauthorization process.

The appointment of Pulte has been met with opposition from both Democrats and Republicans, with many citing his lack of qualifications and concerns about his willingness to "weaponize" the federal government against Trump's critics. Senate leaders have been unable to muster enough votes to pass even a motion to begin debate on the program, and House minority leader Hakeem Jeffries has drawn a hard line over the appointment, calling Pulte "deeply unqualified" and "deeply dangerous." GOP leaders have pressed the White House for an off-ramp, with some suggesting that naming a permanent, Senate-confirmed director could be a way forward.

The standoff has raised concerns about national security, with some warning that the lapse of Section 702 could have significant consequences. However, others argue that the warnings are overblown and that other FISA authorities remain in force. The fight is unfolding as the government withholds records bearing on how Section 702 is used, including a still-secret FISA court opinion that describes serious abuses. Surveillance policy experts are calling for reforms, including a warrant requirement, and arguing that the deadlock could be resolved if leadership allows a vote on these reforms.
