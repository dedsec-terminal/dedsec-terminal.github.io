Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the article:

Russian intelligence services have been hacking into internet-connected security cameras across Europe and Ukraine to gather military intelligence. The cameras are being used to monitor military transport routes, track weapons shipments, and identify the locations of Ukrainian troops. In Ukraine, the surveillance has been used to target and neutralize Ukrainian military personnel and equipment. The hacking is done by scanning the internet for exposed devices, exploiting default passwords and outdated firmware, and using image-recognition software to analyze the video feeds.

The scale of the problem is significant, with over 87,000 internet-connected cameras in the EU, NATO member states, and Ukraine found to be running vulnerable services. In the Netherlands alone, 45,386 cameras are reachable from the public internet, with 1,992 running services with known-exploited vulnerabilities. While not all of these cameras are necessarily hackable, the sheer number of exposed devices provides a large attack surface for Russian intelligence services. The vulnerabilities are often due to default passwords, outdated firmware, and unpatched software, making it easy for hackers to gain access.

To defend against these attacks, organizations are advised to take several steps. These include finding and securing exposed cameras, keeping video streams off the public internet, replacing default credentials, and patching firmware and software. It is also recommended to choose cameras that ship with long-term security support and to aim the lens deliberately to avoid capturing sensitive information. By taking these measures, organizations can reduce the risk of their cameras being hacked and used for military intelligence gathering. The Dutch intelligence services have warned organizations with compromised cameras and are working to prevent further breaches.
