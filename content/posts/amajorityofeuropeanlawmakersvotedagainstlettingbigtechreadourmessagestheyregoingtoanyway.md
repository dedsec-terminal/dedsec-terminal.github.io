The European Parliament has voted to extend legislation allowing tech companies to scan users' private messages for child sexual abuse material, despite a majority of lawmakers voting against the proposal. The ruling, known as "Chat Control" by critics, permits companies like Meta, Google, and Microsoft to voluntarily scan private text, email, and social media messages. However, end-to-end encrypted chats, such as those on WhatsApp and Signal, remain exempt from this scanning.

The European People's Party, the largest political group in the European Parliament, has been pushing to reinstate the legislation, citing the importance of protecting children from online abuse. They argue that tech firms' voluntary detection activities have helped identify and rescue victims of online child sexual abuse. However, civil rights activists and other parties have fiercely opposed the legislation, citing concerns about privacy and the potential for mass surveillance.

The vote was forced through using a procedural maneuver, which skipped preliminary committee debates and required an absolute majority of 361 MEPs to vote against the regulation to block it. Although more members voted against the regulation than for it, they fell short of the required majority by 47 votes. As a result, tech companies will retain the right to scan messages for child sexual abuse detection until 2028, prompting criticism from civil rights activists who argue that the ruling damages democracy and undermines privacy.
