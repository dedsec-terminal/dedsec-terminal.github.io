Bruce Schneier, a renowned technologist and author, reflects on his 2010 column "The Failure of Cryptography to Secure Modern Networks." He argues that cryptography, despite its mathematical strengths, is ill-suited to solve major network security problems such as denial-of-service attacks, identity theft, and viruses. Schneier notes that cryptography has inherent mathematical properties that favor the defender, but computer security is a more balanced and fragile field, with new attacks and defenses emerging constantly.

Schneier's thoughts on cryptography and security date back to the 1990s, when his book "Applied Cryptography" became a bestseller and was even used by NSA cryptographers. He emphasizes that cryptography is a branch of mathematics, but security involves people, relationships, and complex systems. While cryptography is necessary for cybersecurity, it is not sufficient, and other aspects of cybersecurity have become increasingly important as computers and networks have become more pervasive. Schneier's work has stood the test of time, with his books being highly sought after, as evidenced by a commenter who mentions that their copies were "borrowed" and are now valuable collector's items.

The cybersecurity landscape is changing again with the advent of artificial intelligence (AI), which is not advancing cryptography but changing the nature of cybersecurity. AI has demonstrated the ability to find vulnerabilities in software and write exploits, and may soon be able to write patches. This has profound implications for both attackers and defenders, and it is unclear who will win the arms race in a world of "instant software." Schneier's reflections highlight the ongoing evolution of cybersecurity and the need for a nuanced understanding of the complex interplay between cryptography, computer security, and AI.
