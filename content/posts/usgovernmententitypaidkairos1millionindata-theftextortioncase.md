A US government entity, believed to be Union County, Ohio, paid approximately $1 million to a group called Kairos to prevent the release of stolen files. The payment was made in bitcoin and was traced through a series of wallets and cryptocurrency exchanges. The attack did not involve ransomware in the classical sense, as no files were encrypted, but rather the threat of releasing sensitive information if the payment was not made.

The negotiation between Kairos and the government entity lasted about a month, with Kairos initially demanding $3 million and eventually settling for $1 million. The entity started with a low offer of $100,000 and gradually increased it to meet Kairos' demands. The payment was made on June 13, 2025, and Kairos provided a "proof of deletion" file, although it is unclear if the stolen data was actually deleted. This type of attack is becoming more common, with some groups abandoning encryption and using stolen data as leverage to extort payments.

The incident highlights the importance of cybersecurity measures, such as multi-factor authentication, monitoring for suspicious activity, and segregating sensitive data. It also emphasizes the need for a public statement plan and the understanding that promises to delete stolen data are often worthless. The case study serves as a reminder for small government networks to prioritize cybersecurity and be prepared for potential attacks. The fact that Kairos has gone quiet but still has an active wallet suggests that the group may still be operational, and the lessons from this incident can help other organizations prevent similar attacks.
