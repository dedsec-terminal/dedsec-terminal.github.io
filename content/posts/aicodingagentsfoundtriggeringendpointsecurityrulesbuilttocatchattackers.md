Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the article:

Sophos analyzed a week's worth of endpoint data and found that AI coding agents, such as Claude Code and OpenAI Codex, are triggering security rules designed to catch human attackers. These agents are not malicious, but their actions, such as decrypting browser credentials and listing Windows' credential store, are similar to those of attackers. As a result, they are setting off detection rules, including those related to credential access and execution, with 56.2% of blocked activity attributed to credential access and 28.8% to execution.

The analysis revealed that AI agents are using legitimate system tools, such as PowerShell and Windows' built-in Data Protection API, to perform tasks that are also used by attackers. For example, Claude Code's /browse skill uses PowerShell to unlock saved browser data, which triggers a detection rule. Similarly, OpenAI Codex used certutil and bitsadmin to fetch a Python installer, which is a legitimate Windows utility that attackers often abuse. These actions are not malicious, but they are similar to those of attackers, making it challenging for defenders to distinguish between benign and malicious activity.

The findings highlight the need for defenders to re-evaluate their security rules and policies in light of the increasing use of AI coding agents. Sophos recommends splitting rules by what they catch and scoping them to specific agent processes or workspaces to reduce false positives. Additionally, defenders should hold the line on credential-touching behavior, even if it is generated by a benign agent. The company notes that this is an early read on the situation, and the open policy question is what a coding agent should be allowed to touch on an endpoint, with credential stores being a sensible place to draw the first line.
