The United States Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) has released a new directive requiring federal civilian agencies to patch security bugs more rapidly, with some critical cases needing to be fixed within three days. This directive is in response to the increasing threat of artificial intelligence (AI) models that can quickly discover and exploit software vulnerabilities. The goal is to help agencies prioritize and address the most problematic vulnerabilities first, while taking more time to remediate less pressing risks.

The directive establishes a rubric for evaluating patch urgency based on four criteria, including whether a vulnerability is publicly exposed, listed in CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities Catalog, and whether an attacker could automate exploitation. If all four criteria apply, the vulnerability must be fixed within three days, and the agency must also conduct a forensic triage to determine if systems have already been compromised. This new directive supersedes previous orders, which required critical bugs to be patched within 15 days and high-urgency vulnerabilities to be remediated within 30 days.

The directive acknowledges the limitations of federal agencies, including funding shortfalls and competing priorities, and is seen as an initial step to counter the increased capabilities of emerging AI models. However, some experts argue that patching alone is not enough and that the software development community must adopt new, architectural approaches to invalidating whole classes of vulnerabilities at a time. CISA's acting executive assistant director for cybersecurity, Chris Butera, acknowledges that there is still more work to be done, and the directive is seen as a starting point to address the evolving cybersecurity landscape.
