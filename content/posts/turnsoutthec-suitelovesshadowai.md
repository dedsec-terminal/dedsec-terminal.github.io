A recent study by TrustedTech found that senior decision-makers are the heaviest users of unapproved AI tools, also known as shadow AI, despite being aware of the associated security and privacy risks. The study revealed that 65% of decision-makers use shadow AI, compared to 31% of employees below decision-maker level. This suggests that shadow AI is not primarily driven by junior employees, but rather by those in leadership positions who see practical value in using these tools to improve team performance and save time.

The study found that employees continue to use unapproved AI tools because they believe they are essential to their work, with 70% reporting improved team performance and over half saving at least three hours a week. However, many employees are aware of the risks associated with shadow AI, including security and data privacy concerns. The lack of suitable approved alternatives and inadequate training on how to use AI safely and securely are also contributing factors to the widespread use of shadow AI. Interestingly, senior employees are more likely to believe their organization monitors AI use, which may lead them to avoid approved platforms to maintain their professional reputation.

The study highlights the challenges organizations face in controlling employee behavior and enforcing policies around AI use. With senior employees driving shadow AI use, it creates a ripple effect across the organization, making it difficult to implement effective policies. The research also found that employees are often self-learning about AI, with many relying on online resources rather than formal employer training. Furthermore, restrictions alone may not prevent shadow AI use, with nearly one-third of employees saying they would continue using AI tools even if prohibited. This underscores the need for organizations to provide suitable approved alternatives and training on how to use AI safely and securely.
