Professional athletes are facing significant privacy concerns with the increasing use of wearables in their sports. These devices track biometric data, such as heart rate and sleep patterns, which can be used to monitor an athlete's performance and behavior. However, this data can also be used to surveil athletes, potentially infringing on their personal freedom and autonomy. For instance, a coach could use wearable data to check if a player was out partying before a game, or a team could use the data to negotiate a player's contract.

The use of wearable data also raises concerns about commercialization and gambling. Sports leagues are interested in monetizing players' biometric data, which could be used by sharp bettors to gain an advantage. This could lead to a situation where people are betting on not just the outcome of a game, but also on specific aspects of a player's performance, such as their heart rate during a game. Experts warn that this could have serious consequences for athletes, particularly those who are aging or injured, as they may be at risk of having their data used against them.

The issue of wearable data in professional sports is complex and multifaceted. While the data can be beneficial for training and performance, it also raises significant privacy and ethical concerns. The recent labor contract in the WNBA, for example, has been hailed as a victory for players, but it also includes provisions that allow the league to collect and use players' biometric data. This has raised questions about the potential consequences for athletes, including the possibility that their bodies could become "property" of the league. As the use of wearables in sports continues to grow, it is essential to address these concerns and ensure that athletes' rights and autonomy are protected.
