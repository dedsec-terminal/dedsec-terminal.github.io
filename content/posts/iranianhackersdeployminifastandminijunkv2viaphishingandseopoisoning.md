The Iranian state-sponsored threat actor Nimbus Manticore has been attributed to a new campaign targeting organizations in the aviation and software sectors across the U.S., Europe, and the Middle East. The campaign, which began after the joint U.S.-Israeli military campaign against Iran in February 2026, uses phishing and SEO poisoning techniques to deploy new backdoors called MiniFast and MiniJunk V2. The threat actor is affiliated with Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) and has previously targeted defense, aviation, and telecommunication sectors using career-themed phishing lures.

The recent attack chains have shown a shift in tradecraft, with the use of AppDomain hijacking to deliver MiniJunk in February 2026, followed by the deployment of the MiniFast backdoor in March. The threat actor has also used a trojanized Zoom installer and a fake website impersonating a download page for Oracle's SQL Developer software to distribute malware. The MiniFast backdoor is a fully featured backdoor designed for long-term persistence and remote command execution, and it is suspected to have been developed with the assistance of artificial intelligence (AI) tools.

The campaign has been found to be highly personalized, with tailored social engineering tactics, including fake job requisitions and spoofed video conferencing meeting invitations, used to lure victims into initiating the infection chain. The threat actor's use of AI-assisted development and SEO poisoning techniques marks a new approach for the group, and it has been observed that the conflict has accelerated their operations rather than slowing them down. The findings have sparked concerns about the potential risks to critical infrastructure, particularly in the wake of suspected Iranian hacker attacks on gas stations across multiple states in the U.S.
