The article "Chilling Effects" discusses how the Trump administration's tactics have led to a decline in student activism and protests on college campuses. Despite the unpopularity of the administration and its policies, students are self-censoring and disengaging from activism due to fear of punitive measures. This phenomenon is known as a "chilling effect," where people restrain their activities in the face of a threat. The authors argue that this is not an incidental effect, but rather a deliberate strategy by the Trump administration to suppress dissent and opposition.

The chilling effect is not limited to students, but is also evident in other areas, such as academia, media, and law enforcement. Professors are censoring themselves, researchers are avoiding certain topics, and media outlets are modifying their coverage to avoid lawsuits or sanctions. The authors contend that this climate of fear and self-censorship is corrosive to freedom and democracy, and is being used to suppress progressive movements and promote a more conformist and compliant society.

The authors draw historical parallels to the McCarthy era and the Civil Rights Movement, where similar tactics were used to suppress dissent and opposition. They argue that the Trump administration's strategy is not inevitable, and that it can be resisted through legislation, court action, and citizen mobilization. They call on students, teachers, universities, and cultural institutions to resist the tendency to self-censor and conform, and to hold the government accountable for its actions. Ultimately, the authors warn that if left unchecked, the chilling effect could lead to a weakened democratic society and a more authoritarian government.
