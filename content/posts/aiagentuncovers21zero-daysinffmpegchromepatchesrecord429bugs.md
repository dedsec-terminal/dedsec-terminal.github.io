A security startup, depthfirst, has used an autonomous AI agent to discover 21 previously unknown vulnerabilities, or zero-days, in FFmpeg, a widely used media library. The AI agent scanned approximately 1.5 million lines of code and produced 21 confirmed zero-days, each with a reproducible proof-of-concept input, at a cost of around $1,000. Several of the bugs had been latent for 15 to 20 years, with one dating back to 2003.

In a separate development, Google has released Chrome 149, which patches a record 429 security bugs, including over 100 critical or high-severity vulnerabilities. The most severe bug, CVE-2026-10881, allows a crafted page to escape the sandbox and run code on the host. While Google hasn't directly attributed the high number of patches to AI, the company recently overhauled its bounty program to cope with a flood of AI-generated reports. The majority of the high-severity bugs were discovered internally by Google.

The increasing use of AI in vulnerability discovery is putting pressure on developers and maintainers to keep pace with the rapid identification of bugs. While AI has made finding vulnerabilities cheaper and faster, the process of triaging reports, shipping fixes, and getting them installed remains a significant challenge. As a result, there is a growing need for shorter patch cycles, auto-updates, and dependency bumps that prioritize security fixes. The burden of this work often falls on volunteers and a thin layer of human triagers, who must now keep pace with the rapid pace of AI-driven vulnerability discovery.
