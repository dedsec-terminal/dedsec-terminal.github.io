In Q1 2026, the Kaspersky Security Network reported over 2.67 million attacks utilizing malware, adware, or unwanted mobile software. The Trojan-Banker category was the most prevalent mobile malware threat, accounting for 10.86% of total detections. More than 306,000 malicious installation packages were discovered, including 162,275 packages related to mobile banking Trojans and 439 packages related to mobile ransomware Trojans. The number of malware, adware, or unwanted software attacks on mobile devices decreased to 2,676,328 in Q1, down from 3,239,244 in the previous quarter, primarily due to a reduction in adware and RiskTool detections.

The number of Android malware samples saw a slight increase compared to Q4 2025, reaching a total of 306,070. The detected installation packages were distributed by type, with banking Trojans accounting for more than half of all installation packages. The surge in banking Trojan installation packages led to an increase in associated attacks, causing Trojan-Banker apps to become one of the top threats by attack volume. Mamont variants emerged as the most prevalent banking Trojans, accounting for 73.5% of detections. Adware and RiskTool-type unwanted apps remained the top two threats by attack volume, with HiddenAd and MobiDash being the most common adware detections, and Revpn and SpyLoan being the most frequently seen RiskTool apps.

The pre-installed Triada.ag backdoor rose to the top spot in the rankings of most frequently detected types of mobile malware, outpacing even Mamont. Other pre-installed Triada variants also made the rankings, and the Keenadu.a backdoor showed increasing activity. The number of installation packages for mobile banking Trojans detected by Kaspersky increased by 50% compared to the prior quarter, with Mamont variants accounting for the majority of packages. The TOP 10 mobile bankers list was dominated by Mamont variants, which represented nearly every entry in the rankings of most frequent banking Trojans by affected user count.

The report also highlighted the discovery of several apps on Google Play and the App Store that contained a new version of the SparkCat crypto stealer. The Trojan code was meticulously concealed and embedded into the infected Android apps, and the iOS version of the malware underwent several changes, including the use of Apple's proprietary Vision framework for optical character recognition. Additionally, researchers identified a link between the notorious Kimwolf botnet and the IPIDEA proxy network, which was later taken down in cooperation with GTIG. These findings demonstrate the evolving nature of mobile threats and the need for continued vigilance and protection.
