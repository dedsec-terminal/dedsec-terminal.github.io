Meta has been secretly embedding face-recognition technology, known as "NameTag," into its AI app, which has been downloaded over 50 million times. The feature, currently not enabled, is designed to identify people captured by the camera on Meta's smart glasses and alert the wearer when it recognizes someone. The discovery of NameTag contradicts Meta's public statements that it was still "thinking through" the use of face recognition, with the company having previously announced it would delete over a billion faceprints in 2021 due to controversy over its photo-tagging system.

The NameTag system is designed to transform faces into unique biometric signatures, or faceprints, and check them against a database stored on the user's phone. If activated, it will trigger notifications for recognized faces and crop, index, and save unknown faces to a folder. Three AI models powering NameTag have already been deployed to customers' phones, and only traces of the user interface are currently present. Meta's renewed efforts to introduce face recognition come amid mounting opposition from privacy advocates, who argue that it will give stalkers and others easy access to a dangerous technology.

Privacy experts and advocates have expressed concerns over Meta's plans to introduce face recognition, warning that it could normalize a capability that was previously pulled back due to privacy concerns. They argue that embedding face recognition into a mass-market wearable platform could set a dangerous precedent and make it more acceptable for companies to collect and use biometric data without proper consent. Meta has stated that it will take a "thoughtful approach" if it decides to roll out the feature, but critics remain skeptical, citing the company's history of controversy over face recognition and its potential to create a "distributed surveillance machine."
