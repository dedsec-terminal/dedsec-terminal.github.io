As the FIFA World Cup approaches, soccer fans driving to matches in the US may be unaware that they are being watched by automatic license plate reader (ALPR) cameras. WIRED has identified 1,181 ALPRs within a five-mile radius of the 11 US stadiums hosting the World Cup, with most of them manufactured by Flock Safety. These cameras can log license plates, vehicle information, and even descriptions of bumper stickers, creating a dossier of a vehicle's movements.

The use of ALPRs has raised concerns about surveillance and data sharing. Flock Safety allows its customers to share data with other groups on their network, potentially creating a national dragnet. While Flock Safety claims that its customers own and control their data, there have been instances of abuse, including allegations of cops using ALPRs to stalk individuals and accessing data without permission. Other companies, such as Genetec, have expressed concerns about the potential for excessive access to aggregated license plate data.

The prevalence of ALPRs near World Cup stadiums has sparked pushback from communities, with activists fighting for more transparency and legislation to limit the use of ALPRs. Some cities have terminated contracts with ALPR providers, and a national week of action against ALPRs is planned for August. For those attending the World Cup, there are options to avoid ALPR surveillance, including using a navigation app called FlockHopper, which provides routes with the least amount of ALPRs. Alternatively, fans can choose to watch the tournament from the comfort of their own homes, free from surveillance.
