Meta has removed a face-recognition system from its smart glasses app, just one day after WIRED reported on its existence. The system, known as NameTag, was embedded in the Meta AI app and had been downloaded by over 50 million users, despite never being publicly enabled. The feature was designed to convert faces captured by the glasses into unique biometric signatures and compare them against a database of faceprints stored on the user's device.

The removal of the system comes after WIRED's report, which revealed that Meta had quietly integrated the NameTag system into the Meta AI app as early as January. Meta had previously stated that it had made no final decision about face recognition, and executives had dismissed the report as "incredibly misleading" and "absolutely dishonest." However, the company has now removed the face-recognition software, along with the code that ran the NameTag recognition process and the alert that would have been shown if someone were identified.

The removal of the system has been met with criticism from privacy advocates, who argue that it highlights the need for stronger legal protection of consumer privacy. Kade Crockford, director of the technology for liberty program at the American Civil Liberties Union of Massachusetts, stated that the removal didn't undo the original decision to ship the code and that companies like Meta prioritize their bottom line over consumer privacy. Crockford urged lawmakers to step up and protect consumer privacy, particularly with strong enforcement provisions and private rights of action that allow users to sue.
