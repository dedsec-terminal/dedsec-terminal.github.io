A bipartisan amendment is set to be introduced in the US House of Representatives that would prohibit the use of automated license plate readers (ALPRs) for any purpose other than tolling. The amendment, sponsored by Representatives Scott Perry (R-PA) and Jesús "Chuy" García (D-IL), would effectively end the use of ALPRs by state and local law enforcement agencies across the US. 

The amendment would condition federal highway funding on the restriction, which would impact nearly all states, counties, and municipalities that receive federal funding. ALPRs are currently used to track and monitor license plates, logging times and locations, and feeding data into searchable databases shared across agencies and jurisdictions.

Privacy advocates have long warned that the aggregation of license plate data amounts to a de facto warrantless tracking system, and there have been instances of police misuse, including targeting of mosques and disproportionate deployment in low-income neighborhoods. The amendment's sponsors represent opposite ends of the ideological spectrum but converge on the concern over surveillance.

The introduction of the amendment comes amid growing concerns over the use of ALPRs, with some cities and states already banning or restricting their use. A class action lawsuit has been filed against the city of San Jose, California, alleging that its 474-camera network violates residents' Fourth Amendment rights.

If adopted, the amendment would create a spending-power restriction, effectively sidestepping the constitutional issue of whether ALPR queries constitute a Fourth Amendment search. While states reserve the right to decline federal funding, historically, almost none have done so. The amendment is set to be marked up by the House Transportation and Infrastructure Committee on Thursday.
