A bipartisan amendment is set to be introduced in the US House of Representatives that would prohibit the use of automated license plate readers (ALPRs) for any purpose other than tolling. The amendment, sponsored by Representatives Scott Perry (R-PA) and Jesús "Chuy" García (D-IL), would effectively end the use of ALPRs by state and local police departments across the US. 

The amendment would condition federal highway funding on the restriction, which would impact nearly all states, counties, and municipalities that receive federal funding. ALPRs are used to track and monitor license plates, logging times and locations, and feeding data into searchable databases shared across agencies and jurisdictions.

Privacy advocates have long warned that the aggregation of license plate data amounts to a de facto warrantless tracking system, and there have been instances of police misuse, including targeting of mosques and disproportionate deployment in low-income neighborhoods. 

The amendment's introduction comes amid growing concerns over surveillance and privacy, with several cities and states already banning or restricting the use of ALPRs. The move is seen as a significant step towards addressing the issue, and it would side-step the constitutional issue of whether ALPR queries constitute a Fourth Amendment search.

The company that operates the largest ALPR network, Flock Group, has expressed opposition to the amendment, citing the technology's usefulness in law enforcement. However, privacy advocates argue that the risks associated with ALPRs outweigh their benefits, and that the amendment is a "commonsense" measure to protect citizens' privacy. 

If passed, the amendment would mark a significant shift in the national conversation around surveillance and privacy, and could have far-reaching implications for law enforcement and the use of technology in policing.
