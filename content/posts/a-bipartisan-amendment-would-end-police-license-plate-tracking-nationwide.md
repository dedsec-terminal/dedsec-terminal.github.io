A bipartisan amendment is set to be introduced in the US House of Representatives that would prohibit the use of automated license plate readers (ALPRs) for any purpose other than tolling. The amendment, sponsored by Representatives Scott Perry (R-PA) and Jesús "Chuy" García (D-IL), would effectively end the use of ALPRs by state and local law enforcement agencies across the US. 

ALPRs are cameras that photograph license plates, log times and locations, and feed data into searchable databases shared across agencies and jurisdictions. Privacy advocates have long warned that the aggregation of license plate data amounts to a de facto warrantless tracking system, and there have been instances of police misuse, including targeting of mosques and disproportionate deployment in low-income neighborhoods.

The amendment would condition federal highway funding on a ban of ALPR technology, which would force states, counties, and municipalities to either remove the cameras or restructure their use around tolling alone. This move would side-step the constitutional issue of whether ALPR queries constitute a Fourth Amendment search, which has been debated in federal courts.

The introduction of the amendment comes amid growing concerns over ALPR misuse, including a recent lawsuit filed against the city of San Jose, California, alleging that its 474-camera network violates residents' Fourth Amendment rights. The amendment's sponsors represent opposite ends of the ideological spectrum, but they converge on the concern that ALPR networks have become a pervasive layer of American road infrastructure, raising significant privacy concerns.
