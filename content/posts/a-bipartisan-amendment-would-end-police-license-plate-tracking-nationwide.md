A bipartisan amendment is set to be introduced in the US House of Representatives that would prohibit the use of automated license plate readers (ALPRs) for any purpose other than tolling. The amendment, sponsored by Representatives Scott Perry (R-PA) and Jesús "Chuy" García (D-IL), would effectively end state and local ALPR programs across the US by conditioning federal highway funding on a ban of the technology. 

ALPRs, which photograph every passing license plate and log times and locations, have become a pervasive layer of American road infrastructure. However, privacy advocates have warned that the aggregation of license plate data amounts to a de facto warrantless tracking system, and there have been instances of police misuse, including targeting of mosques and disproportionate deployment in low-income neighborhoods.

The amendment's reach would be vast, as it would affect any state, county, or municipality that takes federal highway money, which is essentially all of them. The move comes amid growing concerns over surveillance and the potential for ALPRs to be used to track individuals without their knowledge or consent.

The introduction of the amendment follows several high-profile instances of ALPR misuse, including a Texas sheriff's deputy using the technology to track a woman who had an abortion. Privacy advocates, such as the Electronic Frontier Foundation and Demand Progress, have long warned about the dangers of ALPRs and have called for stricter regulations or bans on the technology.

The amendment's fate is uncertain, but if passed, it would mark a significant shift in the way ALPRs are used in the US. The move would also sidestep the constitutional issue of whether ALPR queries constitute a Fourth Amendment search, which has been the subject of ongoing debate in federal courts.
