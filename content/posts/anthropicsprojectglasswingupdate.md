Anthropic's Project Glasswing, initiated in April, aims to help companies identify and fix vulnerabilities in their software using their new model, Mythos. The project has received significant media attention, with many outlets claiming that Mythos is superior to other models in finding software vulnerabilities. However, this claim has been disputed, and the true effectiveness of Mythos remains unclear.

A recent status report on Project Glasswing reveals that the model has successfully identified numerous vulnerabilities in software, including some that are potentially dangerous. Nevertheless, almost none of these vulnerabilities have been patched, which raises concerns. The lack of transparency from Anthropic, as they refuse to release detailed information about their findings, exacerbates the issue. This has led to speculation about the motivations behind the project and the potential risks associated with their approach.

Some experts have questioned the project's methodology, suggesting that the focus on identifying vulnerabilities without providing patches may be driven by a desire to sell protection services. Additionally, there are concerns about the potential risks of "fast patching" in open-source software, highlighting the need for a more nuanced approach to vulnerability management. As the project continues to evolve, it is essential to critically evaluate the claims made by Anthropic and consider the potential implications of their approach to software security.
