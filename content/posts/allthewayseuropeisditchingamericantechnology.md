Europe is reducing its reliance on American technology due to concerns over data control, changing international relationships, and the close ties between Big Tech firms and the Trump administration. Since the start of President Trump's second term, governments and companies across the continent have accelerated plans to develop home-grown tech and switch to open-source or local alternatives. This shift is driven by a desire for "digital sovereignty" and a need to break free from dependence on US tech firms.

The moves are widespread, with the European Commission launching plans to rely less on US technology, and the European Parliament switching its default search engine from Google to the French alternative Qwant. Thousands of French government workers are using open-source office software, and cities across the Netherlands, France, and Germany are moving away from Microsoft Office and Google Docs. Additionally, the Dutch government is moving its code away from Microsoft-owned Github, and Finland has decided not to move its election data to Amazon's cloud services.

Despite the challenges of entirely unpicking Europe's connections to US technology, the shift is underway. The European entities are driven by a desire to take control of their digital future and untangle themselves from billionaire interests and Trump's policies. While the moves risk inflaming relationships with the Trump administration, European officials believe it is necessary to prioritize digital sovereignty. As a minister in the German state of Bavaria said, "We no longer have time to cheaply discuss the importance of digital sovereignty—given the geopolitical situation, we need to get from talking to doing."
