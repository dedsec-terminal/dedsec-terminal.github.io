The rise of "vibe-coding" platforms has enabled non-technical employees to build and deploy custom applications, often without the knowledge or involvement of IT or security teams. These applications, which can be built in a matter of hours, are frequently connected to sanctioned production systems and published on the open internet, exposing sensitive corporate, operational, or personal data. A recent investigation found over 2,000 such applications, spanning six continents and every industry, with many lacking basic access controls and granting admin access by default.

The traditional security stack is ill-equipped to detect and mitigate these risks, as the applications are custom-built and often published on the open internet, bypassing traditional security controls. Endpoint detection and response (EDR) tools, data loss prevention (DLP) tools, and cloud access security brokers (CASB) are designed to detect and prevent specific types of threats, but they are not effective in detecting and governing vibe-coded applications. The risk lies in the gaps between these tools, and the existing architecture leaves fragments of signal that never assemble into a single, governable picture.

To address this risk, organizations need to adopt a new approach that focuses on visibility and governance at the session layer. This involves discovering and inventorying vibe-coded applications, mapping their connections to corporate systems, and establishing a sanctioned path for employees to build and deploy applications. Continuous discovery and monitoring are also essential, as the landscape of vibe-coded applications is constantly evolving. By taking a proactive and adaptive approach, organizations can reduce the risk of data exposure and ensure that their security posture keeps pace with the rapidly changing threat landscape.
