The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) has proposed a new rule that would effectively eliminate "burner phones" in the US. Burner phones are phones whose accounts are not attached to a specific person, allowing for anonymous use. The proposed rule would require telecom companies to collect and store personal information about all phone customers, including a government-issued ID number and physical address.

The FCC claims that this measure is intended to combat scammers, but privacy advocates and civil rights activists are alarmed by the proposal. They compare it to measures taken in authoritarian countries, where it is difficult to purchase a mobile phone plan without revealing one's identity. The proposed change would significantly impact how people obtain phone plans in the US and could have far-reaching consequences for privacy and cybersecurity.

The collection of personal data would not be limited to individual customers, as telecom companies would also be required to collect information on business and foreign customers, including the intended use of their bulk phone plan purchases and IP addresses. While some argue that this is a necessary measure to prevent scams, others point out that there are alternative ways to maintain privacy, such as using other forms of communication. The proposal has sparked debate, with some countries, like Australia, already having similar rules in place for years.
