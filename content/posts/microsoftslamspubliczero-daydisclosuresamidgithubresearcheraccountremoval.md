Microsoft has expressed strong opposition to public zero-day disclosures, advocating for Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure (CVD) instead. The company believes that sharing vulnerability findings with affected vendors before public disclosure allows them to better understand and address the issues, reducing the risk to customers. This stance comes after a researcher, Chaotic Eclipse, publicly disclosed multiple zero-day vulnerabilities in Windows components, including Defender and BitLocker, without prior notification to Microsoft.

The disclosed vulnerabilities, which include BlueHammer, RedSun, and UnDefend, have already been exploited in the wild, putting Microsoft customers at risk. The company has been working to understand the impact and develop security updates, but emphasizes that uncoordinated disclosures can have "real-world consequences" when proof-of-concept code falls into the wrong hands. Microsoft invites dialogue with the security community and values transparency, but firmly opposes public disclosures that put customers at unnecessary risk.

The researcher, Chaotic Eclipse, has faced backlash for their actions, including the removal of their GitHub account and a subsequent block on a new account created on GitLab. The researcher has responded with criticism of Microsoft's handling of the situation, feeling humiliated and defamed. They have also hinted at releasing new information on July 14, 2026, which they claim will have significant consequences for Microsoft. The incident highlights the ongoing debate between vendors and researchers over the best approach to vulnerability disclosure and the importance of coordinated efforts to protect customers.
