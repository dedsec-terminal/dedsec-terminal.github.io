The alert economy is a significant contributor to security analyst burnout, according to Ido Livneh, CEO of Jazz. The primary cause of burnout is not the long hours, but rather the meaningless work that analysts are tasked with. They spend most of their time closing repetitive tickets, which leads to frustration and dissatisfaction. Furthermore, when senior staff members leave the organization, they take valuable institutional knowledge with them, resulting in a loss of organizational context and an increase in false positives.

The alert economy is characterized by detection tools that flag everything, leaving humans to sort through the alerts. This can be overwhelming, as illustrated by the example of a CISO whose team received 40,000 DLP alert emails in a single week. As a result, the team stopped reading the alerts entirely, highlighting the need for a more effective approach. Livneh argues that the current system is unsustainable and that changes are needed to prevent analyst burnout and improve the overall efficiency of security teams.

To address the issue of analyst burnout, Livneh suggests three potential solutions. Firstly, building tools that understand context before triaging can help reduce the workload and increase efficiency. Secondly, collapsing the traditional L1/L2/L3 tier model into smaller senior teams that own investigations end-to-end can help streamline the process. Finally, creating a technical career track can allow skilled investigators to advance without being forced into management, helping to retain talent and reduce burnout. By implementing these changes, organizations can improve the working conditions and job satisfaction of their security analysts.
