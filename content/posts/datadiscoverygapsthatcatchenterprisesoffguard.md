Avani Desai, CEO at Schellman, discusses the gap between what organizations think they know about their data and what discovery scans actually reveal. She shares examples of companies that thought they had a mature understanding of their data environment, only to discover "shadow data" in abandoned cloud storage, old development environments, and legacy systems. This gap can lead to governance and risk decisions being made based on incomplete information, which can have significant consequences.

Desai also highlights the challenges that can arise during mergers and acquisitions when data inventory becomes a deal issue. She cites an example of a company that discovered duplicated datasets across multiple inherited systems, without clear retention policies or defined ownership, after a deal closed. This led to a painful remediation effort, including customer notifications, operational disruption, and significant costs. Desai notes that smaller companies often outperform larger enterprises on compliance and modernization due to their agility, clearer ownership, and fewer legacy systems.

Desai identifies synthetic data as over-marketed, as it can create a false sense of security if underlying governance issues are not addressed. On the other hand, she believes confidential computing is underappreciated, as it provides the ability to protect data in use, not just at rest or in transit. To test a company's claim of having a current data map, Desai asks who is accountable for validating it after operational changes, which often reveals that the data map is not as up-to-date as claimed. This highlights the need for continuous operational discipline to maintain a current data map.
