Spotify has been quietly removing tens of thousands of podcasts that advertised illegal online pharmacies over the past year. A report by Senator Maggie Hassan's office faults the company for acting only after news outlets exposed the content and her office pressed for answers. The report states that Spotify removed over 57,000 podcast episodes and 3,000 shows, and took enforcement action against 3,500 accounts, but none of the removed content was sent to law enforcement.

The report criticizes Spotify's moderation efforts, citing a significant jump in the number of accounts removed for drug content in 2025 compared to the previous year. Spotify attributes this increase to a change in its tracking methods, but the report suggests that the company only took action after coming under scrutiny. The removed podcasts often contained links to illegal online pharmacies, which could be accessed by clicking on links in episode descriptions and cover art. However, Spotify does not track these clicks, and the report warns that counterfeit pills bought online can be laced with deadly fentanyl, posing a significant risk to teenagers and others.

The problem of fake podcasts and search-manipulation spam is not unique to Spotify, with similar content found on other platforms such as iHeart, Amazon Music, and Podchaser. The report highlights the need for online platforms to deploy sophisticated efforts to identify and remove illegal content, particularly in the age of AI-generated content. Spotify has built defenses against AI spam for music, but not for podcasts, and the company has stated that it is not well-positioned to identify AI-made content. The report calls for greater cooperation between online platforms and law enforcement to combat the spread of illegal content and protect users from harm.
