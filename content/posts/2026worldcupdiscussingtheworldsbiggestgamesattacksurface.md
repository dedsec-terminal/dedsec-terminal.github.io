The 2026 FIFA World Cup, jointly hosted by the US, Canada, and Mexico, will be the largest sporting event in history, with 104 matches and an estimated 5-6 million in-venue spectators. The tournament's complex network infrastructure, which relies on municipal services such as public transit, power, and emergency services, creates a vast attack surface for potential cyber threats. A review of past mega-events reveals a high likelihood of disruptive intrusions, criminal fraud, and politically motivated attacks, with Iran-nexus activity, Russia-nexus hacktivism, and financially motivated cybercrime being the primary drivers of risk.

The threat landscape for the 2026 World Cup is characterized by the potential for cybercriminals to target fans and the hospitality supply chain, as well as the risk of Iran-nexus disruptive operations against US infrastructure and pro-Russian hacktivist attacks against host-city, federation, and ticketing services. Previous attacks against major international sporting events, such as the Rio and Pyeongchang Olympics, demonstrate the potential for significant disruption and highlight the need for robust cybersecurity measures. The use of fake domains, mobile applications, and social media accounts to commit ticket fraud, accommodation scams, and other types of cybercrime is also a significant concern.

To mitigate these risks, defenders should plan for a range of potential threats, including cybercriminal attacks on fans and the hospitality supply chain, Iran-nexus operations against US infrastructure, and pro-Russian hacktivist attacks against host-city services. The use of phishing, malware, and other social engineering tactics to commit cybercrime is also a significant concern. By understanding the threat landscape and taking proactive measures to secure the tournament's infrastructure and protect fans, the 2026 World Cup can be a successful and secure event.
