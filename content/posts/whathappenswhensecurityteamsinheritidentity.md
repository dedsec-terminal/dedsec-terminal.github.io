Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the conversation with Eric Woodruff, Chief Identity Architect at Semperis:

Many organizations still treat identity as an IT hygiene problem rather than a strategic risk category. This perception often changes after a security incident, when identity becomes a reactive rather than proactive concern. However, there is a growing trend of security teams taking ownership of identity, which can help shift the culture and perspective around identity. Despite this, the industry still has a long way to go, and it may take time for organizations to fully understand the risks associated with identity.

Identity platforms can be complex and difficult to manage, especially for small and medium-sized businesses that may not have the resources to dedicate to identity management. The platforms themselves can be challenging to secure, and the standards are not always easy to understand. As a result, many organizations struggle to properly secure their identity environments, and mistakes can happen. Additionally, the industry has been slow to adopt phishing-resistant authentication methods, such as passkeys, due to concerns about user confusion and the perceived difficulty of implementation.

The rise of agentic AI and non-human identities (NHIs) is creating new challenges for identity management and security. Currently, most agentic systems are designed to act on behalf of a human, and there is a lack of standards and guardrails to control their actions. The industry is working to develop solutions, but in the short term, endpoint controls and restrictions may be necessary to limit the potential risks. While AI has the potential to be a powerful tool for both good and bad, it is still too early to tell what the ultimate impact will be on cybersecurity, and it is important to separate fact from fear and marketing hype.
