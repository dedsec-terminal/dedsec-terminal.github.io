Google's Threat Intelligence Group (GTIG) has significantly disrupted NetNut, a residential proxy network that spans over 2 million home devices worldwide, including smart TVs and streaming boxes. NetNut allows strangers to route their traffic through a user's internet connection, making it appear as ordinary home browsing and potentially leading to malicious activities. The network is estimated to have been used by over 300 distinct threat clusters, including cybercriminal and espionage groups, to hide their real location and run password-guessing attacks.

NetNut is owned by Alarum Technologies, a publicly traded Israeli company, which claims that its software is for consented bandwidth-sharing and does not compromise devices. However, researchers have found that the company's apps do not provide clear consent prompts to users, and the traffic path suggests that NetNut is indeed a botnet. Google's intelligence aligns with the researchers' findings, and the company has worked with the FBI, Lumen, and others to reduce the network's pool of usable devices by millions.

To protect themselves, consumers are advised to be cautious of apps that offer to pay for "unused bandwidth" or "sharing internet," and to stick to official app stores and known manufacturers. Additionally, users should keep built-in protections like Google Play Protect switched on and be mindful of the permissions requested by VPN or proxy apps. Google notes that taking down one network is not enough, as operators can simply resell their capacity under different brand names, and that a more comprehensive approach is needed to disrupt these networks and prevent them from resurfacing under different names.
