Meta, the parent company of Facebook, hired hundreds of contractors to pose as minors online and test how rival chatbots responded to sensitive and high-risk topics such as suicide, sex, and drugs. The project, known as Cannes, targeted chatbots from OpenAI, Google, and Character.AI, and involved creating dummy under-18 accounts and sending written prompts and images to the chatbots. The contractors were instructed to copy the responses into spreadsheets, which were then reviewed by Meta.

The prompts used in the testing were often designed to push the chatbots toward responses that their safety systems were supposed to refuse. The testing included hundreds of prompts focused on suicide and self-harm, eating disorders, sex, and romance, as well as others involving drugs, profanity, and racial slurs. Many of the prompts were written from the perspective of children or teenagers in crisis, and some were crude or repetitive attempts to elicit responses that a well-functioning chatbot should plainly reject. The project raised questions about what it measured beyond the systems' ability to refuse obvious provocations.

Meta defended the project as routine safety testing, but former contractors who worked on the project described it as alarming and potentially problematic. They raised concerns about generating or preserving child sexual abuse material and secretly taking material from competitors' systems to feed into Meta's own system. Experts also questioned the scale and opacity of the project, as well as the lack of disclosure to the companies being tested. The project appears to have violated the terms of service set by the competitors, and the companies involved have stated that they did not authorize the testing and that it violated their policies.
