The concept of "cybersecuritization" refers to the tendency of policymakers to frame various issues as cybersecurity threats, thereby granting them a sense of urgency and exceptionalism. This phenomenon, also known as "mission creep," allows issues such as misinformation, social media safety, and anti-sex trafficking statutes to be repositioned as existential threats, rather than important but non-essential problems. By doing so, these issues gain access to a unique set of politics and laws that can lead to troubling governance responses.

Cybersecuritization can have negative consequences, including oversimplification of complex problems and the invitation of unidimensional solutions. This can lead to the use of "argumentative trump cards," such as First Amendment challenges, and the deference to purported specialists and their proposed solutions. As a result, governance choices become more opaque, eroding public trust and political legitimacy. The article highlights the need to analyze and critique cybersecuritization, demonstrating its insidiousness and the likelihood of its continued expansion.

The article's analysis and critique aim to reclaim the hard work of governance and prevent the abdication of responsibility to the "trump card" of cybersecurity. By examining cases from various domains, the article surfaces the phenomenon of cybersecuritization and offers a novel framework for understanding and addressing it. The goal is to promote more nuanced and informed decision-making, rather than relying on the simplistic and potentially damaging approach of cybersecuritization. Ultimately, the article seeks to promote a more transparent and accountable governance process, one that prioritizes the rule of law and equal justice over the interests of special groups or individuals.
