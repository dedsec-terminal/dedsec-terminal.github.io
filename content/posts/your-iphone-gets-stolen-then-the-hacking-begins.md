Millions of phones are stolen every year, and while some are sold for parts, others are unlocked and resold for a higher profit. Researchers at cybersecurity firm Infoblox have uncovered a thriving ecosystem of software sellers that provide "unlocking" tools and technology to help gain access to stolen iPhones. These tools are sold on the web and on Telegram, with prices averaging below $10, and are used to unlock devices, often through phishing attacks. The researchers have tracked dozens of groups selling these tools and linked over 10,000 phishing websites to the activity, with traffic to these domains increasing by 350% last year.

The Infoblox researchers found that the unlocking tools often come with features such as jailbreaking older iPhones, pulling owner information from phones, and launching phishing attacks to collect unlocking information. The tools can also wipe the device by default as soon as access is attained, allowing thieves to resell the phone. The researchers identified multiple groups on Telegram pushing these "unlocking" services, which can be used to access Apple iCloud accounts and remove them from phones. The services often include phishing kits that mimic Apple's Find My iPhone feature, allowing thieves to trick owners into revealing their PIN codes.

The stolen phone market is a lucrative one, with unlocked phones worth significantly more than locked ones. According to Dan Guido, CEO of security firm Trail of Bits, a stolen phone may only be worth $50 to $200 when it is locked, but can be worth $500 or $1,000 if it is unlocked. This has led to the development of a complex ecosystem of thieves, unlockers, and resellers, all working together to unlock and resell stolen phones. The Infoblox researchers found that the services do not explicitly mention use for stolen devices, but the incorporation of phishing tools within some services indicates they are likely not meant for legitimate purposes.

The Infoblox researchers were able to track the phishing domains and identify the groups selling the unlocking tools by creating DNS fingerprints for the phishing domain they had received and tracking other related Apple look-alike websites. They also found that some of the groups were using software called iRealm to generate phishing links and pages mimicking Apple services. The researchers were able to obtain a video showing the software in action, and found that it could be used to launch phishing attacks and collect unlocking information. The London Metro Police's Will Lyne advises phone owners to take simple steps such as activating built-in anti-theft features, keeping software updated, and using strong passwords to reduce the harm caused if a phone is stolen.

The issue of stolen phones and the ecosystem of unlocking services is a complex one, and it will likely continue to evolve as phone security improves.
