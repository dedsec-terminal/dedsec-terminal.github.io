US law enforcement agencies are warning of a growing threat from "anti-tech extremism" as protests against data centers and AI technology increase. Federal intelligence agencies and domestic law enforcement have obtained over 1,000 pages of reports highlighting the emerging threat, which includes attacks on CEOs, nationwide protests, and concerns about AI job replacement. The reports, obtained by WIRED, show a national shift in surveillance efforts to target individuals and groups deemed a threat to the tech industry.

The new focus on anti-technology extremism follows President Donald Trump's National Security Presidential Memo 7, which instructs the Department of Justice to target individuals with "anti-American," "anti-Christian," and "anti-capitalism" beliefs. The Trump administration's counterterrorism strategy has also identified left-wing extremists as a top priority. The reports warn of widespread upheaval in response to AI adoption, with potential for large-scale protests and civil unrest. A new term, "anti-tech violent extremism," has been coined to describe the emerging threat, which encompasses a range of ideologies under a single extremist category.

Critics argue that the surveillance efforts are overly broad and could target peaceful protesters and activists. The reports list vaguely defined activities as suspicious, including photography, observation, and testing of security. Legal experts say that these activities could be carried out by anyone, and that the reports are often based on unreliable information. The monitoring of constitutionally protected events and demonstrations has also raised concerns about the erosion of civil liberties. Open-source intelligence companies are also scouring the web for anti-technology sentiment, which has led to allegations of targeting individuals based on their views rather than actual threats.
