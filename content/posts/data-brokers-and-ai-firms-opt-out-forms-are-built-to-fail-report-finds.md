The Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC) has released a report detailing the deceptive methods used by major data-collecting companies to prevent consumers from opting out of the sale and sharing of their personal information. The report found that 38 major data companies, including Google, Meta, and OpenAI, use manipulative design to make it difficult for users to opt out, with tactics such as burying links in fine print, requiring multiple forms to be completed, and demanding that users create accounts or pay for subscriptions before opting out.

The report highlights the case of people-search data brokers, such as Spokeo, Whitepages, and National Public Data, which do not offer a way for consumers to opt out of the sale or transfer of their data. Instead, these companies provide a process for removing individual listings by URL, one at a time, with no commitment to stop selling the same person's information in the future. This can have serious safety implications, particularly for individuals such as domestic violence survivors, public officials, and their families, who may need to remove their information from circulation to protect themselves from harm.

EPIC's researchers found that many companies, including Bumble, default users into data sharing through preselected toggles, while others, such as OpenAI, do not clearly link their opt-out forms from their homepages or privacy policies. Some companies, including Palantir and TikTok, do not provide an option to opt out of the sale or sharing of personal data at all. In response to the report, some companies, such as Amazon and OpenAI, have disputed the findings, claiming that they do not sell customer personal information or that they provide straightforward ways for users to control how their data is used.

The report argues that the opt-out process is often the only mechanism available to remove a home address from circulation before someone shows up at the door, and that companies must design opt-out processes with respect toward consumers' rights. EPIC concludes that the real remedy is not better forms but less collection, with rules that bar companies from gathering personal information they never needed in the first place. The report's findings highlight the need for stronger regulations to protect consumer privacy and prevent the misuse of personal data.

The companies implicated in the report have responded in various ways, with some disputing the findings and others acknowledging the need for improved opt-out processes. However, the report's conclusions emphasize that consumers cannot effectively protect their own privacy by exercising opt-out rights, and that a more fundamental approach is needed to address the issue of data collection and sharing. Ultimately, the report suggests that the only way to truly protect consumer privacy is to limit the amount of personal information that companies are allowed to collect in the first place.
