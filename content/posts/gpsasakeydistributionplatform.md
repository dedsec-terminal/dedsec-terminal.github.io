The US military has been utilizing GPS satellites as a key distribution platform for its global encryption network, effectively turning each satellite into a "numbers station". This means that every device using GPS has been receiving hidden government information for nearly 20 years, without the general public's knowledge. Researcher Steven Murdoch discovered this by analyzing GPS signals and cross-referencing declassified documents, which revealed a match between the timeline of signal transmissions and the rollout of the military's Over-the-Air Distribution (OTAD) and Over-the-Air Rekeying (OTAR) systems.

The use of GPS for key distribution allows military GPS receivers to be rekeyed remotely, replacing the need for manual distribution of cryptographic keying material. This automated system enables secure communication and has been in operation since at least 2011. The discovery of this hidden function has significant implications, as it highlights the military's ability to utilize public infrastructure for secure communication. The fact that this has gone unnoticed for so long raises questions about the security and transparency of GPS technology.

The use of GPS for key distribution also raises concerns about the longevity of secure modules used in military GPS units. These modules have limited capacity for storing key material, which means they will eventually need to be reloaded or replaced. This limitation precludes the use of certain secure systems, such as one-time pads (OTPs), and raises questions about when these secure modules will reach the end of their shelf life. As Clive Robinson notes, the finite capacity of these modules means that they will eventually need to be updated or replaced, which could have significant implications for the security of military communication systems.
