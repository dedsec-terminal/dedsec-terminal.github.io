In 2025, a significant number of AI-related laws were enacted, with 145 laws passed and over 1,000 bills introduced or revised. This surge in legislation has put a strain on privacy teams, who are struggling to keep up with the increasing complexity of AI regulations. According to DataGrail's report, many businesses are exposed to "shadow AI risks" due to the lack of transparency in AI subprocessors, with 63.6% of popular business software providers failing to disclose this information.

The report also highlights the challenges of opt-out compliance, with 63% of websites failing to honor opt-out signals despite being required to do so by law. Additionally, the number of data subject requests has increased for the fifth consecutive year, with medium-sized companies spending an average of $1.5 million per year to handle these requests manually. The rise of AI has also led to an increase in risk assessment requirements, with California making privacy risk assessments a legal requirement starting this year. Organizations must conduct these assessments and submit the results for annual audits, with each review requiring personal attestation from a company executive.

To manage the growing complexity of AI regulations, privacy teams are turning to AI-powered tools to support their work. Despite headcount reductions of up to 33%, organizations plan to use AI to help with privacy-related tasks. DataGrail's CEO, Daniel Barber, notes that the only way for privacy teams to keep up with the increasing demands is to invest in "privacy-first AI tools" that can help them scale their programs intelligently and stay ahead of regulation. With the volume of data subject requests, new AI laws, and enforcement actions showing no signs of slowing down, privacy teams must adapt to the changing landscape to ensure compliance and deliver secure AI to their businesses.
