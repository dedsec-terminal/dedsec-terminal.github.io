Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the article:

Meta, the company behind Facebook and Instagram, has been developing a face-recognition system called NameTag for its smart glasses. The system was included in the Meta AI app, which has been downloaded tens of millions of times, but was not active and could not be used by users. Despite this, Meta's executives have been arguing that NameTag does not exist, with Vice President of Communications Andy Stone claiming that the feature is not available and therefore does not exist.

However, an analysis of the Meta AI app code by WIRED and independent experts found that the NameTag system was technically functional and could be used to recognize faces. Additionally, Meta's CTO Andrew "Boz" Bosworth described the feature in detail on a podcast, saying it would allow users to identify people they have met before. This contradicts Meta's claims that the feature does not exist, and the company has been trying to downplay the contradiction by emphasizing that Bosworth said the feature "would be" great, not that it is or will be.

The debate over whether NameTag exists is not just semantic, as it has implications for Meta's compliance with state biometric laws. By keeping face-matching on the user's phone rather than on a central server, Meta may be trying to argue that it complies with laws such as the Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA) in Illinois. However, the legality of this approach is contested, and courts have issued conflicting rulings on whether companies can be said to "possess" biometric data stored on users' devices. Ultimately, the question of whether NameTag exists is a matter of interpretation, but it is clear that Meta has designed and built a face-recognition system and is considering enabling it.
