Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the changes to GitHub's bug bounty program:

GitHub is making significant changes to its public bug bounty program, effective July 27, 2026. The company is reducing payout amounts by at least half at every severity level, with critical findings now fixed at $10,000, down from $20,000-$30,000+. The new payout structure includes fixed payments of $250 for low-severity findings, $2,000 for medium, $5,000 for high, and $10,000 for critical vulnerabilities. Reports filed before the change will retain the previous payout terms.

The changes aim to reduce noise and give established researchers faster responses, higher rewards, and closer access to GitHub's security engineering team. A new VIP tier will offer higher rewards, with payments of $1,000 for low-severity findings, $7,500 for medium, $20,000 for high, and $30,000 or more for critical vulnerabilities. Researchers can qualify for the VIP program by reporting a certain number of vulnerabilities, but the announcement does not specify the time window or guarantee an invitation. GitHub says the changes will remove uncertainty and triage overhead, and may still award discretionary bonuses for exceptional work.

The changes come as AI-generated bug reports are becoming more common, and GitHub is adapting to this new landscape. The company welcomes AI-assisted security research, but emphasizes that the quality of the work, not the tools used, is what matters. The restructuring may suppress automated noise, but it may also make it harder for new researchers to enter the program. The invite-only VIP structure may improve speed and report quality, but it may also narrow the range of people examining the platform, one of the main advantages of a public bounty program.
