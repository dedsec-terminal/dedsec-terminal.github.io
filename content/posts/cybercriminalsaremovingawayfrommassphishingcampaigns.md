Cybercriminals are shifting away from mass phishing campaigns, with a 20% decline in phishing activity in both 2024 and 2025, according to Zscaler's ThreatLabz team. Instead, they are using targeted phishing campaigns that resemble routine business communications, with the services sector experiencing a 65.5% year-over-year increase in phishing activity. These campaigns often use legitimate services like OneDrive and target users with billing notices, onboarding documents, and other business-related themes.

The use of AI-generated website instances is becoming a significant part of phishing operations, with ThreatLabz identifying over 413,000 instances, of which 37,447 were classified as malicious. These AI-generated sites can be used to create phishing pages, fake applications, and brand impersonation sites, making it easier for attackers to launch phishing campaigns. Additionally, more than 95% of phishing activity is now delivered over encrypted channels, making it harder to detect. Researchers also found that 87% of all malicious activity blocked in 2025 was delivered over HTTPS.

Attackers are becoming more sophisticated, using techniques like adversary-in-the-middle (AiTM) and browser-in-the-middle (BiTM) to intercept login sessions and capture credentials, MFA codes, and session tokens in real-time. They are also using cloud infrastructure to fuel scanning activity, with over 121,000 AWS-hosted IP addresses probing customer environments. Zscaler expects phishing campaigns to become even more automated in 2026, with AI agents targeting other AI agents and attacks moving between different channels, making it essential for organizations to stay vigilant and adapt their security measures to counter these evolving threats.
