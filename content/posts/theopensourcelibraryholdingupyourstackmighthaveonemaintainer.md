The open-source software ecosystem is diverse and complex, with many different types of projects and maintainers. Researchers have identified 14 sub-genres of open-source software, each with its own characteristics, such as community-driven projects, company-backed projects, and foundation-governed projects. These categories can predict how a project will behave in production, particularly in terms of funding and governance. For example, a community-driven project like Debian or Arch Linux relies on volunteers and merit-based authority, while a company-backed project like Elastic or MongoDB is controlled by a single firm that earns revenue from the software.

The type of project can have significant implications for its sustainability and reliability. For instance, a solo project maintained by one person can be fragile and vulnerable to "bus factor" risk, where the project grinds to a halt if the maintainer walks away. In contrast, a project backed by a foundation with paid staff and member dues can be more resilient. The researchers also found that open-source projects for social good, such as health-records systems or crisis-mapping tools, tend to have a more stable and dedicated community of contributors. Understanding the type of project and its characteristics can help organizations make informed decisions about which open-source tools to use and how to mitigate potential risks.

The categorization of open-source projects can help organizations assess the risks associated with using open-source software. For example, an open-core product from a single vendor may come with relicensing risk, while a solo package may come with bus-factor risk. A foundation-backed project, on the other hand, may offer more stability and governance. By understanding the type of project and its characteristics, organizations can make more informed decisions about which open-source tools to use and how to manage the associated risks. This knowledge can help organizations ensure the reliability and sustainability of their software stack and avoid potential pitfalls.
