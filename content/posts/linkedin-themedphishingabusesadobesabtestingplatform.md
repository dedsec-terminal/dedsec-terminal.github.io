A new phishing campaign is targeting professionals with fake LinkedIn business emails, abusing a trusted service operated by Adobe. The attack starts with an email that appears to be a routine business inquiry, with a signed contract attached for review. The email is short and professional, with a sender company and name that exist, making it seem legitimate.

When the attachment is opened, a familiar-looking LinkedIn login page appears, with the victim's email address already filled in. If the victim enters their password and submits, they will be redirected to the real LinkedIn page, while their login credentials are sent to a server operated by the attackers in the background. The attackers use several layers of deception, including disguising the attached HTML file as a PDF, pre-filling the email field, and abusing Adobe's A/B testing platform to make the attack seem legitimate.

To avoid falling victim to these attacks, users should be cautious when opening unsolicited attachments and enable multi-factor authentication for critical accounts. It's also recommended to access accounts only through official apps, by typing the official website directly into the browser, or via a bookmark. These attacks are cheap, scalable, and likely to continue circulating, making it essential for users to be vigilant and take necessary precautions to protect their accounts. By being aware of these phishing warning signs, users can reduce the risk of falling victim to these types of attacks.
