The Pentagon has been warned for nearly a decade that commercially available location data could be used to track and target American troops. Despite these warnings, the department has failed to take adequate measures to protect its personnel. A newly disclosed letter from US Central Command confirms that adversaries are exploiting commercial location data to target US personnel in the Middle East. This is the first official acknowledgment that the data-broker economy is being used to hunt American forces in the region.

The warnings about the dangers of commercially available location data date back to 2016, when a government technologist demonstrated how commercial location data could be used to track phones from US military bases to covert forward operating bases in Syria. Since then, numerous reports and investigations have highlighted the risks posed by the data-broker industry. In 2023, researchers at Duke University found that they could purchase data on American service members, including names, home addresses, and health conditions, for as little as 12 cents a record. The same data was available to anyone with a credit card, including foreign intelligence services.

The failure to address the issue has led to a situation where American troops are being targeted using commercially available location data. Lawmakers are now calling on the Pentagon to take action, including disabling advertising IDs on military phones, pulling Google Chrome from government devices, and enrolling service members in state data-broker opt-out systems. The incident highlights the need for comprehensive privacy legislation to regulate the data-broker industry and protect national security. As one privacy advocate noted, "surveillance is not inherently good for security," and the public can now see disturbing evidence that privacy is critical to keeping people safe.
