The FBI is seeking to acquire nationwide access to automated license plate readers (ALPRs) across the US, with the goal of obtaining "near real-time" data on vehicle movements. This move has been revealed through recently published procurement records, which show the agency planning to pay millions for access to data captured by roadside ALPRs. These cameras take images of every passing vehicle, adding their license plate, location, time, and data into searchable databases that are often accessed by local law enforcement agencies and some federal agencies.

The FBI's statement of work emphasizes the need for accessible ALPRs to provide a diverse and reliable range of collections across the US, with data available across major highways and in various locations for maximum usefulness to law enforcement. This development comes as US lawmakers have proposed legislation to prohibit the use of ALPRs for police tracking, highlighting the ongoing debate around surveillance technology and privacy concerns. The FBI's plans have significant implications for individual privacy and the potential for mass surveillance, raising questions about the balance between law enforcement needs and civil liberties.

In related news, Google has published a live exploit code for an unpatched vulnerability in Chromium, the open-source codebase underlying several popular web browsers. The flaw, which was originally reported 42 months ago, allows any website to spin up a persistent service worker on a device, potentially enabling the monitoring of browsing activity, routing traffic through the victim's machine, or pulling the device into a proxied DDoS network. Google has flagged the bug as serious and is working on a fix, but users are advised to be cautious and treat unexplained download windows as suspect.

The US government has also taken steps to crack down on deepfake sexual abuse, with the Federal Trade Commission sending warning letters to companies offering nudifying services and the Department of Justice arresting two men for allegedly sharing AI-created photos and videos of real women. These developments reflect growing concerns about the misuse of artificial intelligence and the need for stronger regulations to protect individuals from online harassment and exploitation.
