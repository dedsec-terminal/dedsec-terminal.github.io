The US House of Representatives has released a bill to reauthorize a surveillance program that allows federal agents to read Americans' communications without a warrant. The program, known as Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA), has been criticized for its use in spying on racial justice protesters, journalists, and members of Congress. Despite the controversy, the new bill does not address the core issue of warrantless searches, and its oversight provisions are largely seen as cosmetic.

The bill requires the FBI to provide written justifications for its queries of the 702 database, but this provision is weakened by the fact that the office responsible for reviewing these justifications has limited staff and no authority to suppress improper queries. Additionally, the attorneys responsible for reviewing the queries can be summarily fired for flagging improper activity, which undermines their ability to provide effective oversight. Other provisions in the bill, such as the threat of prison time for FBI employees who violate querying rules, are also seen as ineffective due to the high standard of intent required for prosecution.

Critics of the bill argue that it does not provide meaningful constitutional safeguards and that its provisions are designed to appease lawmakers who are on the fence about reauthorizing the program. A senior Democratic aide referred to one of the provisions as a "legislative scam" that is intended to dupe lawmakers into supporting a bill that has no real teeth. The bill's supporters, including Representative Jim Himes, argue that the program is essential for national security and that there is no evidence of abuse under the current administration.

The bill's prospects are uncertain, with some lawmakers, including Senator Ron Wyden, strongly opposing it. Former Republican House Judiciary chair Bob Goodlatte has also expressed disappointment with the bill, saying that it does not provide real impediments for FBI agents who want to search Americans' private communications. The debate over the bill is likely to continue, with many lawmakers and advocacy groups pushing for more meaningful reforms to the surveillance program.

The controversy surrounding the bill highlights the ongoing tension between national security concerns and individual privacy rights. As the bill moves forward, it is likely to face significant scrutiny and opposition from those who argue that it does not do enough to protect Americans' constitutional rights. The outcome of the debate will have significant implications for the future of surveillance in the United States and the balance between security and liberty.
