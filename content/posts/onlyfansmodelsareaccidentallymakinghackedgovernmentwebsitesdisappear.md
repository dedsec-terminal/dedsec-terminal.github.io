Adult content creators on platforms like OnlyFans have been filing millions of copyright infringement requests to protect their work from being pirated and shared online. However, in doing so, they have inadvertently helped to remove hacked government and university websites from search results. According to a report by cybersecurity company UpGuard, over 2,000 domains belonging to governments and education institutions across 80 countries have received copyright takedown requests linked to adult content creators over the past 15 years.

These requests have been made by companies representing adult content creators, who are trying to remove stolen pictures and videos from search results. However, the hacked government and university websites have been using the names of adult content creators to lure victims to their compromised pages, which often link to scams or malware downloads. The copyright takedown requests have been effective in removing these pages from search results, but some experts argue that this is not the right solution to the problem. They say that copyright laws are being misused to clean up hacked official sites, and that a more targeted approach is needed to address the issue.

The issue has been exacerbated by the rise of adult content creators and the increasing use of DMCA takedown requests to protect their work. While the intentions of the creators are legitimate, the unintended consequences of their actions have been to help remove hacked government and university websites from search results. Experts say that a more nuanced approach is needed to address the issue, one that balances the need to protect intellectual property with the need to prevent the misuse of copyright laws. Meanwhile, adult content creators like Laura Lux say they are not surprised that their names and brands are being used in this way, and that they will continue to take steps to protect their work online.
