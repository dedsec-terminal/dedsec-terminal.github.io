Here is a summary of the article in three concise paragraphs:

The recent introduction of Anthropic's Mythos, an AI-driven vulnerability discovery tool, has raised concerns about the potential for increased vulnerabilities and breaches. However, the article argues that the real issue is not the number of vulnerabilities, but rather the "exposure window" - the time gap between when a vulnerability becomes exploitable and when it is fixed. This window is currently too wide, with attackers able to move quickly (with an average breakout time of 29 minutes) while organizations take much longer to respond (with an average remediation time of 55 days for high and critical vulnerabilities).

The exposure window is kept open by "mobilization" - the process of owning, remediating, and coordinating the fix for a vulnerability. This process is often slow and cumbersome, involving multiple teams and approvals, and is not keeping pace with the speed of attackers. The article notes that while vulnerability management models have improved, with automated discovery and prioritization, the mobilization stage is still a bottleneck. Recent policy moves, such as CISA's BOD 26-04, acknowledge the need for faster remediation, but do not address the underlying mobilization problem.

To close the exposure window, security teams need to adopt speed-based metrics, such as those used by Security Operations Centers (SOCs), and focus on reducing the "blast radius" - the set of critical assets that can be reached by an attacker. This involves prioritizing remediation based on exploitability and business impact, and using attack path analysis to identify the most critical vulnerabilities. By doing so, teams can narrow the scope of mobilization and reduce the exposure window, ultimately reducing the risk of breaches. The article concludes that Mythos did not break security programs, but rather highlighted the need to address the existing exposure window and mobilization challenges.
