Researchers at UC Irvine sent deletion and opt-out requests to 322 data brokers in California to test their compliance with the state's privacy laws. The results showed that most brokers failed to respond to the requests, with around 70% not reporting the outcome of deletion requests and only about 26% responding to opt-out requests. This lack of transparency leaves consumers uncertain about whether their personal data has been deleted or protected.

The study also found that some brokers were non-compliant with the law, with around 22% asking for identity verification for opt-out requests, which is prohibited by the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA). Additionally, some brokers asked for more personal information than necessary, and a few even sent marketing emails to the addresses tied to the made-up identities used in the study. In some cases, brokers provided incorrect or misleading responses, such as confirming a deletion and attaching a sample of consumer records that contained real people's information.

The study's findings highlight the challenges consumers face in exercising their privacy rights under the CCPA. However, a new tool called the Deletion Request and Opt-out Platform (DROP) is set to launch in August 2026, which will allow California residents to file a single request to opt-out of data sales and deletions across all registered brokers. This platform may improve the response rates and compliance of data brokers, but for now, consumers outside of California are still required to contact each broker individually or pay a removal service to do so.
