The concept of "Mythos" refers to a potential threat in the software industry, where AI-generated vulnerabilities can be chained together to create severe security risks. Despite some skepticism, the author believes that Mythos is real and poses a significant threat to the industry. The US government is aware of the issue but is struggling to regulate it, as open-source software is not easily governable. The author suggests that the focus should be on consumption, rather than creation, of open-source software.

The current open-source ecosystem is not equipped to handle the scale and complexity of AI-generated vulnerabilities. Many companies consume open-source software without fully understanding the risks, and maintainers of open-source projects are often overwhelmed by automated scanners and AI-generated reports. The author argues that a new approach is needed, including a coordinated disclosure process and a "maintainer of last resort" to handle vulnerabilities that cannot be patched by the original maintainers. This would involve centralizing the maintenance of forks of critical projects, which would require significant funding, staffing, and trust.

The author presents three possible scenarios for addressing the issue: doing nothing and hoping for the best, allowing a chaotic and fragmented approach to emerge, or taking a deliberate and coordinated approach to build new trust infrastructure for open-source consumption. The author advocates for the latter, which would involve building a new system for disclosing and patching vulnerabilities, as well as maintaining forks of critical projects. This would require significant effort and resources, but the author believes it is necessary to ensure the long-term security and sustainability of the open-source ecosystem.
