The Walt Disney Company has introduced face recognition technology at its Disneyland Park and Disney California Adventure Park, allowing visitors to opt-in to the system when entering the parks. The technology works by converting facial images into numerical values, which are then used to match faces in other images. Disney claims that these numerical values will be deleted after 30 days, except in cases where data must be maintained for legal or fraud-prevention purposes. This move is part of a broader trend of face recognition systems being used in various aspects of life, including law enforcement, airports, and sports stadiums.

The use of face recognition technology raises concerns about privacy and data protection. While Disney's system is optional, visitors who choose not to use it may still have their image taken if they enter the park through lanes without face recognition systems. This highlights the need for clear guidelines and regulations around the use of face recognition technology, particularly in public spaces. As the technology becomes more widespread, it is essential to ensure that individuals' rights to privacy and data protection are respected.

In other cybersecurity news, the National Security Agency (NSA) is testing Anthropic's Mythos AI tool, which is designed to discover hackable bugs in software. The tool has been limited to 40 organizations, and the NSA has used it to hunt for bugs in Microsoft's software. The NSA's adoption of the tool is notable, given the Department of Defense's declared ban on Anthropic due to supply chain risk concerns. Meanwhile, a 19-year-old alleged member of the Scattered Spider ransomware group has been arrested, and a Medicare database has been found to have exposed health care providers' Social Security numbers and other personal information.
