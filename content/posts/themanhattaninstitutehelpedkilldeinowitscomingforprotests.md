The Manhattan Institute, a right-wing think tank, is behind state-level legislative efforts to classify minor protest-related crimes as "civil terrorism." The institute, cofounded by former CIA director William Casey, is pushing for laws that would reclassify crimes like vandalism, blocking a roadway, or trespassing during a protest as felonies with 18-month prison sentences. This effort is part of a broader campaign to crack down on leftist organizations and social movements, and to recast acts of nonviolent civil disobedience as potential crimes.

The Manhattan Institute's legal policy fellow, Tal Fortgang, is the main proponent of the "civil terrorism" theory, which he argues is a form of terrorism that involves mass commission of minor crimes to intimidate or coerce a population. Fortgang claims that he focuses on anti-war, pro-Palestinian, and Black Lives Matter activists because they constitute the majority of groups engaged in this behavior. He believes that states should step up protest-related crimes from misdemeanors to felonies because when hundreds of people gather to commit disorderly conduct together, it becomes a form of civil terrorism.

Two pieces of state-level legislation, ghostwritten by the Manhattan Institute, have been introduced in Utah and Arizona. Utah's law, which was passed earlier this year, heightens penalties for "aggravated disorderly conduct" during protests and creates a new crime for "unlawfully advancing foreign organizations." Arizona's bill, which is currently awaiting a vote in the state Senate, would make it a felony to block a public thoroughfare and would outlaw civilians wearing masks at protests. Critics argue that these laws are an attack on First Amendment rights and are designed to have a chilling effect on free speech and assembly.
