LG Electronics USA has announced plans to suspend smart TV apps that turn users' TVs into always-on residential proxy nodes. This decision comes after research by the security firm Spur found that over 42% of games and apps on LG's webOS store contain residential proxy software development kits (SDKs) that allow unknown third-parties to route internet traffic through a user's TV. These SDKs can be used to monetize apps, but they also pose a risk to users' privacy and security.

The research by Spur also found that over a quarter of apps on Samsung's Tizen operating system had similar residential proxy components. LG has stated that it is working with app developers to remove the residential proxy option from their apps, and those that fail to comply will have their apps suspended. The company is committed to keeping residential proxy networks out of its smart TV apps and is strengthening its evaluation process for developer-submitted apps. This move is seen as a positive step towards protecting users' privacy and security.

The issue of residential proxy networks is not limited to LG, as other proxy providers such as Bright Data have been found to be involved in similar practices. These companies claim to follow rigorous know-your-customer processes and incorporate technological countermeasures to prevent misuse, but critics argue that the risk of embedding these networks in devices that consumers do not think of as computers is too great. LG's decision to ban residential proxy SDKs from its app store is a welcome move, but the company has also faced criticism for other questionable partnerships, such as promoting McAfee security products through software drivers in its high-end LCD monitors.
