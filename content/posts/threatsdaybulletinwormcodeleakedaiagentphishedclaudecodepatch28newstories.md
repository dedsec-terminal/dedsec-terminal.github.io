Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the ThreatsDay Bulletin:

The cybersecurity landscape has seen a significant escalation in threats, with a supply chain attack kit being made publicly available and a $5,000-a-month remote access trojan (RAT) being sold on darknet forums. The RAT, known as SilabRAT, is capable of bypassing security measures and stealing credentials, with a focus on financial gain. Additionally, research has shown that AI agents can be tricked into leaking real credentials, highlighting the growing sophistication of cyber threats.

Several notable incidents have been reported, including the exposure of 3.3 billion identity records, with over 11.1 million devices infected with infostealers in 2025. A North Korean threat actor, Famous Chollima, has been responsible for 47% of state-sponsored hands-on-keyboard operations against the tech sector. The US Department of Justice has also seized 13 internet domains used to target US persons, including current and former security clearance holders, with promises of easy money. Furthermore, a cross-platform RAT called SStar Agent has been discovered, with the ability to monitor and exfiltrate files on both Windows and macOS systems.

Other notable threats include the Miasma credential-stealing attack framework, which was briefly made available for free on GitHub, and the discovery of a weakness in Microsoft Exchange that could allow attackers to send spoofed emails. A technique called "download pumping" has also been observed, where attackers artificially inflate npm package download counts to make malicious packages appear legitimate. Additionally, a previously unknown group known as SiribClone has been targeting Russian military personnel with phishing waves, using bait applications and posing as women seeking romantic relationships to distribute malicious files. These incidents highlight the increasing complexity and sophistication of cyber threats, and the need for vigilance and robust security measures.
