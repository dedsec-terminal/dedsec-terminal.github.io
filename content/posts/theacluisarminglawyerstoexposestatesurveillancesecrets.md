The American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) of Massachusetts is releasing an online toolkit to help criminal defense attorneys uncover whether police used surveillance technologies to build cases against their clients in secret. The toolkit includes handcrafted legal motions that can force prosecutors to reveal the use of surveillance technologies, such as facial recognition, automatic license plate readers, and gunshot detection systems. These motions are designed to be used in conjunction with a bedrock rule of American criminal law that requires prosecutors to turn over all evidence that could help the defense.

The ACLU's goal is to arm defense lawyers with the tools they need to expose the use of surveillance technologies in court, particularly in cases where the use of these technologies may have been hidden from defendants and their attorneys. The toolkit also includes preservation motions, which demand that surveillance data be saved before it is deleted automatically, targeting government agencies and private vendors alike. This is a critical issue, as many surveillance technologies have automatic deletion features, which can erase evidence that could be used to exonerate defendants.

The ACLU's effort is part of a broader campaign to regulate the use of surveillance technologies and ensure that their use is transparent and subject to constitutional safeguards. The group is fighting on two fronts: pressuring cities to drop unregulated surveillance tools and arming defense lawyers to expose their use in court. The toolkit's authors believe that by providing defense attorneys with the tools they need to challenge the use of surveillance technologies, they can help build a record of how these technologies are being used and create a foundation for future challenges to their legality.
