Malcolm Portelli, CISO at Coinflow, a crypto payment firm, emphasizes that the company's threat model is driven more by the industry it operates in, rather than its location. As a result, Coinflow is a prime target for advanced persistent threat groups due to its involvement in financial services, Web3, and crypto. Portelli notes that crypto is a big target for these groups, as it is their preferred method of payment. To mitigate these risks, Portelli has implemented various security measures, including multi-factor authentication mechanisms for API keys.

Portelli has also reevaluated his approach to security awareness training, moving away from monthly videos that had become a compliance exercise. Instead, he now prefers quarterly training sessions, capped at 30 minutes of content per quarter, supplemented with formats designed to hold attention. When communicating with the board, Portelli uses data and numbers to convey the risks and financial exposure associated with cyber threats. He cites published reports, such as the Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report and the IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report, to help board members understand the financial implications of cyber risks.

Looking ahead, Portelli expects the attack volume to continue climbing over the next three years, driven by AI tools that can find vulnerabilities at a low cost. He is investing in AI-based anomaly detection and pattern recognition to flag suspicious transactions and is also focused on educating employees and end users about the shifting nature of fraud. Additionally, Portelli highlights the need for automated patching that preserves application functionality, as defensive AI has kept up with vulnerability discovery, but remediation tooling lags behind. He also advocates for retiring outdated security guidance, such as forced password rotation, which he believes has outlived its usefulness.
