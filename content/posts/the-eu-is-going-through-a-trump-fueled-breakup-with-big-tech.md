The article discusses how the European Union, particularly France, is accelerating its efforts to reduce its dependence on US technology due to concerns over data security, the Trump administration's unpredictability, and changing prices. The French government is developing and deploying its own technology for government officials, including a video-calling platform called Visio, and a set of productivity tools called "LaSuite". The goal is to shift to French, European, and open-source technology alternatives, with data being stored locally in the country.

The article highlights that:

* Over 40,000 French government staff have started using the home-grown video platform Visio, and the rest will move away from Zoom, Microsoft Teams, and others by 2027.
* The French government is working with other countries and private firms on the development of its tools, and is aiming to lead by example in reducing its reliance on US tech.
* Other European countries, such as the Netherlands, Austria, Belgium, Denmark, and Finland, are also making efforts to reduce their dependence on US technology.
* The French efforts are not limited to the central government, with cities like Lyon also moving away from American technology and towards open-source alternatives.
* The article cites experts who say that while it's not possible to completely eliminate US tech from European systems, open-source technology and digital public infrastructure could be a way to reduce dependence on US companies.

The article also mentions that the EU's dependence on US tech companies is significant, with:

* 70% of the EU market using Google, Microsoft, and Amazon's cloud services.
* 80% of European company software spending going to US firms.
* At least 23 countries depending on US tech firms for "critical national security functions".

Overall, the article suggests that the EU, particularly France, is taking steps to reduce its reliance on US technology and promote digital sovereignty, but acknowledges that it's a complex and challenging process.
