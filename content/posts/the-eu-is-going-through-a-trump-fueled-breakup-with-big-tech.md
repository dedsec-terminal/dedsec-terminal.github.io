The article discusses how the European Union, particularly France, is accelerating its efforts to reduce its dependence on US-based technology due to concerns over data security, the Trump administration's unpredictability, and changing prices. The French government is developing and deploying its own technology for government officials, including a video-calling platform called Visio, and a set of productivity tools called "LaSuite". The goal is to shift to French, European, and open-source technology alternatives as much as possible, with data being stored locally in the country.

The article highlights that:

* Over 40,000 French government staff have started using the home-grown video platform Visio, and the rest will move away from Zoom, Microsoft Teams, and others by 2027.
* The French government is working with other countries and private firms on the development of its tools, and is based on open-source software.
* The Dutch government has moved its open-source code off of GitHub and onto a Forgejo instance hosted on government-owned servers.
* Other European countries, such as the Netherlands, Austria, Belgium, Denmark, and Finland, are also making efforts to reduce their reliance on US technology.
* The French efforts are not limited to the central government, with cities like Lyon also moving away from American technology and towards European or open-source software.

The article also notes that:

* The movements in Europe have accelerated due to the Snowden revelations, unlawful EU-US data sharing agreements, and the Trump administration's actions.
* Nextcloud, a company that creates video conferencing and work tools, has seen a threefold increase in potential new customers due to concerns about US services.
* The Cloud Act, which gives US law enforcement the ability to request access to data stored on servers internationally, is a major concern for European countries.
* Open-source technology might be one way of moving away from US tech companies, but it is not a complete solution, and European countries will likely still rely on US technology to some extent.

Overall, the article suggests that the European Union is taking steps to reduce its dependence on US technology, but it will be a challenging and ongoing process.
