The article discusses how the European Union, particularly France, is accelerating its efforts to reduce its dependence on US-based technology due to concerns over data security, the Trump administration's unpredictability, and changing prices. The French government is developing and deploying its own technology for government officials, including a video-calling platform called Visio, instant messaging app Tchap, and productivity tools called "LaSuite". The goal is to shift to as many French, European, and open-source technology alternatives as possible in the coming years.

The move is part of a broader trend in Europe, with other countries such as the Netherlands, Austria, Belgium, Denmark, and Finland also pushing for digital sovereignty. The French government has already started using its own technology, with over 40,000 government staff using Visio and 420,000 active users of Tchap.

The article highlights the concerns over US tech companies' ability to exert control over European data and systems, citing the example of the International Criminal Court's chief prosecutor losing access to his email hosted on Microsoft Outlook. The Cloud Act, which gives US law enforcement the ability to request access to data stored on servers internationally, is also a concern.

While open-source technology is seen as a way to move away from US tech companies, the article notes that it is not possible to completely eliminate dependence on US tech. European countries still rely heavily on US firms for critical national security functions, and US companies dominate the software market.

The article concludes that the European efforts to reduce dependence on US tech are driven by a desire for digital sovereignty and control over their own technology, but it is a complex and challenging process. The use of open-source technology and collaboration with other countries and private firms are seen as key steps towards achieving this goal.
