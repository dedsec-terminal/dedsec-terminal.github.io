The AI era is transforming the bug hunting landscape, with autonomous AI models identifying software vulnerabilities and developing exploits at an unprecedented rate. This has led to a surge in bug submissions, changing the economics of bug bounties for both institutions and researchers. Tech giants like Google and Apple have increased their bug bounty rewards, with Apple's top reward rising to $2 million last year. However, this abundance of bug submissions is also flooding vulnerability disclosure programs, making it challenging for companies to handle the pressure.

The increased use of AI in bug hunting is not only changing the dynamics of the industry but also creating new challenges for organizations. Attackers are also leveraging AI to discover novel vulnerabilities and create exploits, forcing companies to quickly release patches. The 90-day disclosure deadline, a standard in the industry, may no longer be sufficient, and companies may need to deploy vulnerability fixes more rapidly. The use of AI by attackers is becoming more sophisticated, with Google researchers observing prominent cybercrime threat actors using AI tools to bypass two-factor authentication on an open-source system administration platform.

The bug hunting industry is evolving, with some companies ending their bug bounty programs due to low-quality submissions generated by AI. However, others are adapting, with Google overhauling its Vulnerability Reward Programs and increasing payouts for more challenging vulnerabilities. Researchers argue that structural defenses are necessary to address accelerating vulnerability discovery, and companies need to build infrastructure that makes bugs irrelevant. As the industry continues to evolve, it is likely that bug hunting will become more automated, and human researchers will need to develop specialized skills to remain relevant.
