Dialog, a private network cofounded by Peter Thiel, has been secretly ranking its members based on their wealth, fame, and influence. The organization uses a hidden scale, assigning grades of A, B, or C to its attendees, with the "C" grade reserved for the most famous and influential individuals. This ranking system is used to determine who attends events, who they are seated with, and even who is charged what price to attend. The grades are reassessed after every retreat, and members can be disinvited from events if their grade falls too low.

The ranking system is based on a variety of factors, including wealth, fame, and the individual's "value-add" to the group. Staff notes attached to member dossiers provide insight into the grading process, with comments such as "Small AUM" (assets under management) or "widely recognized within his field" but not a leader of "an organization that is a household name." The algorithm used to assign grades fixates on whether the "average person" would recognize someone, and measures them against "a Fortune 500 company or a top celebrity." The grades are also used to determine who is paired with whom for networking and dating purposes.

The leaked data also reveals that Dialog tracks its members' apparent political leanings, with staff making separate internal assessments that do not always match the members' self-described leanings. The data shows that women account for roughly a third of those graded, but hold only 18 percent of top marks. The leak also points to a built-in matchmaking system, with over three-quarters of members having a list of algorithm-suggested matches. Despite Dialog's claims of being nonpartisan and nonpolitical, the data suggests that the organization's ranking system and matchmaking process may be influenced by factors such as wealth, fame, and political leanings.
