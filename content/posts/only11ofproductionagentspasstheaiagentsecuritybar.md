A recent independent assessment of 100 production AI agents found that only 11% of them pass the AI agent security bar. The AI Risk Quadrant (AIRQ) report scored these agents across three dimensions: attack surface, blast radius, and defense controls. The results showed that nearly all of the agents carry the conditions for a single hostile document to take them over, with 98% of them having a "lethal trifecta" of private data access, exposure to untrusted content, and the ability to take outbound actions.

The report identified the two riskiest categories of agents as coding agents and computer-use agents, which have the widest attack surfaces and largest blast radii with the thinnest defenses. In contrast, Work Copilot and Business Process agents are among the most heavily defended classes. The report also found that 37% of the agents score well on logging and observability but poorly on defense components, and that 83% of claimed defenses lack independent verification. The assessment highlights the need for better security controls and verification processes to prevent potential risks associated with AI agents.

The report recommends that buyers treat the agent as the unit of risk, compare agents within the same class and quadrant, and score every platform twice - once as the vendor ships it and once as the customer configures it. It also suggests that procurement gates should include documented and tested sandboxing to reduce residual risk. The AIRQ methodology is designed to be open, usable, and reproducible, and the report recommends quarterly re-audits to stay on top of the evolving AI agent market and its associated risks. By following these guidelines, organizations can better mitigate the risks associated with AI agents and ensure a more secure adoption process.
