A group of cryptographers, including former developers of the end-to-end encrypted messenger Signal, has introduced "Encrypted Spaces," a system for creating private collaboration apps. The goal is to enable end-to-end encryption in complex, multiuser software like Slack, Discord, and Google Docs, where users can collaborate, make changes, and invite or remove participants while maintaining strong encryption protections. This new approach moves beyond the traditional "pipe" metaphor of end-to-end encryption, where data is encoded for two users, to create "spaces" where multiple users can interact securely.

The Encrypted Spaces team has released a preview of their open-source code libraries, which utilize new cryptographic techniques like zero-knowledge proofs to enable computers to manipulate and verify encrypted data without accessing its contents. This allows for a centralized server to manage data and changes while keeping it encrypted, and users can collectively make changes to the information while maintaining encryption protections. The system also enables features like change logs, zero-knowledge roll-ups, and key management, making it easier for developers to build end-to-end encrypted apps without needing extensive cryptography knowledge.

The Encrypted Spaces project has the potential to unleash a new generation of end-to-end encrypted apps, providing a standardized approach to building secure collaboration tools. While some may raise concerns about the potential misuse of such apps by malicious actors, the developers argue that this shouldn't stand in the way of creating platforms that better protect everyone's privacy by default. With the release of their code repository, the Encrypted Spaces team invites cryptography researchers and developers to review and contribute to the project, aiming to make end-to-end encryption the norm for collaborative software.
