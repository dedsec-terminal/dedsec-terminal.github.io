The US government has acknowledged the widespread use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) to automate government processes, with 3,611 active or planned use cases across federal agencies. This represents a 70% increase from the previous year and includes applications in areas such as public health, nuclear reactor safety, and law enforcement. Some examples of AI use cases include the Department of Veterans Affairs using AI to assess the mental state and suicide risk of callers to the veterans crisis line, and the Department of Energy testing AI to control nuclear reactors.

While some of these use cases may raise concerns about the transfer of decision-making power from humans to machines, others may have bipartisan support, such as using AI to help veterans at risk of self-harm. However, the lack of transparency and public consultation in the development and implementation of these AI systems is a significant issue. The disclosures provided by the government are often brief and lacking in context, making it difficult for the public to understand the purpose and approach of these systems.

To address public concerns and build trust, the US government should adopt more transparent and participatory approaches to AI development and deployment. This could include algorithmic impact risk assessment procedures and public comment processes, similar to those used in countries like Canada and France. By facilitating open dialogue and consultation, the government can ensure that the use of AI in government agencies is safe, trusted, and equitable, and that it improves the efficacy, efficiency, and accessibility of government services.
