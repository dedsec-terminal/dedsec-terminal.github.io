Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the article on synthetic identity fraud and its implications for machine identities:

Synthetic identity fraud is a type of identity theft where an attacker creates a new, fake identity by combining real and fabricated data points. This concept has a parallel in the realm of non-human identities (NHIs), where attackers can create fake machine identities that blend in with legitimate ones. These fabricated machine identities can accumulate permissions and credibility over time without being detected, as there is no real owner to monitor their activity. Security teams often focus on protecting NHIs from being stolen, but the creation of fake machine identities is a largely unexplored threat.

Fabricated machine identities can be created through various techniques, including rogue service accounts, DCShadow attacks, and shadow credentials. These fake identities can inherit the environment's naming conventions, exist in the correct domain, and carry plausible-looking metadata, making them convincing and difficult to detect. The lack of attention to this threat makes it particularly dangerous, as fabricated machine identities can evade detection and accumulate permissions without raising any alarms. The growing number of NHIs in enterprises, combined with weak governance and lack of human ownership, creates an environment where fake machine identities can thrive.

To defend against synthetic machine identities, organizations need to implement strong governance and security measures. This includes assigning ownership to every NHI, rotating secrets, enforcing least privilege, and continuously verifying behavior. By doing so, organizations can eliminate the potential for fabricated identities to blend in and accumulate permissions. Additionally, using an identity security platform can help manage ownership, secrets, and privileged access, making it more difficult for attackers to create and exploit fake machine identities. As machine identity creation becomes increasingly automated, it is essential for organizations to prioritize machine identity security and stay ahead of this emerging threat.
