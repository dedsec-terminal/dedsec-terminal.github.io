The UK government plans to introduce facial age estimation technology to determine the age of asylum seekers arriving at the border. This technology uses AI to scan a person's face and estimate their age. However, an investigation by WIRED and Lighthouse Reports has found that the technology is flawed and contains serious bias problems. The system regularly mistakes children for adults, and its accuracy varies depending on the person's race, gender, and demographic details.

The investigation obtained an internal UK government report that details the testing of seven facial age estimation algorithms. The report found that the system performed significantly worse when used to estimate the ages of Sub-Saharan Africans compared to other groups. For female Sub-Saharan Africans, the estimated age was off by an average of 4.6 years, meaning that a 13.5-year-old girl could be assessed as an 18-year-old adult. The report also found that the system's accuracy depends on the quality of the photos taken, with poor-quality images leading to larger errors.

The use of facial age estimation technology in asylum seeker age assessments has raised concerns among experts and rights groups. They argue that the technology is not reliable enough to be used in high-stakes scenarios, where incorrect age assessments can have serious consequences for asylum seekers. The Home Office has defended its plans, saying that the technology will be used as an additional tool to support border officers' judgments, but critics argue that the technology's flaws and biases make it unsuitable for use in this context. Rights groups, including Foxglove, have called on the UK government to scrap its plans to use the technology, citing concerns about its inaccuracy and potential for racist bias.
