Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the article:

A small team of around a dozen analysts at Slovenia's national cyber response center, SI-CERT, handles approximately 6,000 cyber incidents a year. The team, led by Gorazd Božič, has been in operation since 1994 and has grown from a small group of three people to its current size. The center records a significant increase in incidents, from around 300 ten to fifteen years ago, and sorts them into three triage lines: routine reports, serious incidents, and phishing reports.

The team uses the ENISA reference taxonomy for security incidents to classify each case, labeling the incident type, victim's sector, and adding free-form tags to feed the center's statistics. SI-CERT has earned the trust of the private sector, particularly the banking, energy, and telecommunications sectors, by providing valuable services such as phishing site takedowns and malware analysis. The center works closely with the police, providing deep network knowledge and malware analysis, and has a malware analysis lab to support its work.

Despite its small size, SI-CERT plays a crucial role in helping organizations respond to cyber incidents. Božič emphasizes the importance of cooperation and information sharing, and notes that the center's role is often misread as an inspectorate or law enforcement branch. He is skeptical of AI claims and automated security operations centers, believing that human analysts are still necessary to understand the meaning of alerts. SI-CERT's budget is justified each year, and the center presses for more funding to support its growing workload and new requirements, such as vulnerability handling duties under the CRA directive.
