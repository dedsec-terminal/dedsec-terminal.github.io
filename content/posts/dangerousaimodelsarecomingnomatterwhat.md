The US government has restricted access to Anthropic's new AI models, Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5, due to concerns that they could be used by malicious actors to exploit software vulnerabilities. Anthropic had warned that its models had advanced capabilities for both defensive and offensive purposes, and had taken steps to limit their use, such as releasing a version with blocks on certain topics. However, the government believes that these safeguards can be bypassed, posing a national security risk.

Despite the restrictions, experts say that the development of similar AI models by other companies and open-source developers is inevitable, and that the current measures are only delaying the inevitable. They argue that the focus should be on developing broader and more transparent plans for contending with advances in AI capabilities, rather than trying to restrict specific models. Researchers note that existing AI offerings can already be used for advanced vulnerability-hunting and exploit development, and that smaller, cheaper, and open-source models can match the performance of more advanced models with sophisticated prompting.

The incident highlights the need for a more nuanced approach to regulating AI, one that takes into account the dual-use nature of the technology and the potential risks and benefits. Experts say that the policy question should not be whether a technology has risk, but whether specific restrictions can meaningfully reduce that risk, or whether they mainly slow down efforts to make systems safer. As AI capabilities continue to advance, governments and industries will need to work together to develop more effective and transparent strategies for mitigating the risks and harnessing the benefits of these technologies.
