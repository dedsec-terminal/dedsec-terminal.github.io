Security budget waste often arises from a mismatch between how budgets are allocated and how attackers operate. Budgets are typically built around vendor categories, compliance requirements, and last year's security threats, whereas attackers exploit vulnerabilities along specific attack paths. This mismatch leads to inefficiencies and waste in security spending.

Two significant sources of security budget waste are overlapping tools and shelfware. Overlapping tools refer to the duplication of efforts, where multiple tools flag the same issue multiple times, resulting in unnecessary costs. Shelfware, on the other hand, refers to security solutions that are purchased but not fully utilized, covering only a portion of the estate at full invoice cost. Additionally, hidden costs such as triage hours, alert fatigue, and false confidence can further exacerbate the issue.

To address security budget waste, Viktor Bulanek proposes a five-step plan that can be implemented within a quarter. The plan involves mapping tool overlap, measuring the number of findings that are proven exploitable, cutting or consolidating tools, redirecting savings towards continuous validation of real attack paths, and tracking the cost per validated exploitable finding fixed before production. By following this plan, teams can optimize their security spending and make the most of their existing budget, rather than simply requesting more funds.
