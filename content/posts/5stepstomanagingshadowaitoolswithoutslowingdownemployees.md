The increasing use of AI tools by employees has created a "shadow AI gap" where security teams have limited visibility into the tools being used and the data they access. This gap is widening as employees use more AI tools, often without IT review, to increase productivity. According to Gartner, 69% of organizations suspect or have confirmed that employees are using prohibited AI tools at work, and only 37% have an AI governance policy in place.

To manage this gap, organizations can follow a five-step program. The first step is to build a full picture of which AI tools are in use, including those connected through OAuth, browser extensions, and AI features bundled inside already-approved tools. The second step is to write a policy that works with employees, providing a list of approved tools, data classification rules, and a process for requesting new tools. The third step is to create a fast lane for new tool requests, using a structured intake form and defined evaluation criteria to remove friction from the approval process.

The final two steps involve using monitoring as a shared safety layer to provide real-time visibility into AI tool usage and making good security behavior easy through just-in-time coaching and training. By following these steps, organizations can create a security program that works with employees, providing them with the tools they need while minimizing the risk of data exposure. This approach can help close the shadow AI gap and reduce the incentive for employees to work around the system, ultimately leading to a more secure and productive work environment.
