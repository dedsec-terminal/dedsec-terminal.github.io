Kimsuky, a Korean-speaking threat actor, has been observed using new malware variants based on the PebbleDash platform, which was originally used by the Lazarus Group. The group's latest campaigns have shown notable tactical shifts, with the use of VSCode tunneling, Cloudflare Quick Tunnels, DWAgent, large language models, and the Rust programming language. Kimsuky has been targeting organizations in South Korea, as well as in Brazil and Germany, with a focus on the defense sector. The group's malware, including HelloDoor and httpMalice, is designed to establish backdoors and steal information, and has been distributed through spear-phishing emails and other means.

The PebbleDash cluster, which includes HelloDoor and httpMalice, is considered one of the most technically advanced in Kimsuky's toolset. These malware variants are designed to evade detection and have been used to target a range of sectors, including defense, government, and medical organizations. HelloDoor, for example, is a Rust-based backdoor that uses a C2 server hosted through TryCloudflare, a temporary tunneling service provided by Cloudflare. httpMalice, on the other hand, is a more recent variant that uses a combination of HTTP and Dropbox communications to interact with its C2 server.

Kimsuky's use of legitimate tools, such as VSCode and DWAgent, has also been observed. The group uses these tools to establish persistence and conduct post-exploitation activities, such as remote monitoring and management. The group's ability to adapt and evolve its tactics and tools has made it a significant threat to organizations in the region. The use of large language models to generate code has also been observed, with comments in the code suggesting that it was generated by an LLM service rather than a human developer.

The group's initial access to target systems is typically gained through spear-phishing emails containing malicious attachments disguised as documents. The attachments often consist of compressed files containing droppers in formats such as JSE, EXE, PIF, or SCR. The droppers are designed to deploy malware, including the PebbleDash and AppleSeed clusters, which are considered the most technically advanced in Kimsuky's toolset. The group's ability to use a range of tactics and tools has made it a significant threat to organizations, and its continued evolution and adaptation are likely to pose ongoing challenges to cybersecurity professionals.

Kimsuky's targeting of South Korean entities, as well as organizations in Brazil and Germany, suggests that the group's interests are focused on the defense sector and other strategic industries. The group's use of PebbleDash and AppleSeed malware clusters has been particularly effective in establishing backdoors and stealing information, and its ability to adapt and evolve its tactics and tools has made it a significant threat to organizations in the region. Overall, Kimsuky's activities pose a significant threat to cybersecurity, and its continued evolution and adaptation are likely to pose ongoing challenges to cybersecurity professionals.
