A 23-year-old Canadian man, Jacob Butler, also known as "Dort", has been arrested and charged in both the US and Canada for allegedly building and operating the Kimwolf botnet. The botnet, which infected millions of devices, was used to launch massive distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks over the past six months. The attacks targeted various organizations, including the US Department of Defense, and resulted in financial losses exceeding $1 million for some victims.

Butler was identified as the alleged botmaster by cybersecurity researcher Brian Krebs, who had been targeted by Dort in a series of DDoS, doxing, and swatting campaigns. The investigation involved the analysis of IP addresses, online account information, transaction records, and online messaging application records.

Butler faces charges in Canada, including unauthorized use of a computer, possession of a device to obtain unauthorized use of a computer system, and mischief in relation to computer data. In the US, he faces one count of aiding and abetting computer intrusion, which carries a maximum sentence of 10 years in prison.

The case highlights the growing threat of IoT botnets and the importance of international cooperation in combating cybercrime. The arrest of Butler is seen as a significant development in the fight against DDoS attacks and other forms of cybercrime.

The article also sparks a discussion about the severity of the sentence that Butler should face, with some commenters arguing that he should receive a harsher sentence due to the scale of the attacks and the potential harm caused. Others raise concerns about the potential leniency of the sentence, given Butler's age and lack of prior criminal history.
