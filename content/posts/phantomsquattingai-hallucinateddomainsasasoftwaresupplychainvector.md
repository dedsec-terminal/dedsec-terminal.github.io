Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the concept of Phantom Squatting:

Phantom squatting is a new software supply chain attack vector that exploits the tendency of large language models (LLMs) to hallucinate web domains for legitimate brands. Adversaries are registering these nonexistent domains to intercept traffic generated by AI systems, posing a significant risk to the software supply chain. Unit 42 researchers have found that LLMs consistently generate fictitious domains, which can be used to launch phishing attacks, steal sensitive information, or compromise systems.

The attack lifecycle of phantom squatting involves four phases: Discover, Act, Lure, and Bypass. In the Discover phase, attackers probe LLMs to identify hallucination patterns and map a target brand's phantom domains. In the Act phase, adversaries register these domains before defenders can react. The Lure phase involves using the LLM as an unwitting attack delivery mechanism, where the AI system recommends the fictitious domain to users or autonomous agents. Finally, the Bypass phase relies on the zero-reputation status of the newly registered domain to evade conventional URL defenses.

To detect and mitigate phantom squatting, organizations can use advanced security products and services, such as Advanced WildFire, Advanced URL Filtering, and Prisma AIRS. The Unit 42 AI Security Assessment can also help empower safe AI use and development. Additionally, proactive monitoring of registration for high-priority hallucinated domains can yield real-world detections and predict adversary registration. By understanding the risks and mechanisms of phantom squatting, organizations can better protect themselves from this emerging threat and ensure the security of their software supply chain.
