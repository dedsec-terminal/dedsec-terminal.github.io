Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the article:

The article discusses the importance of combining autonomous AI with human analysts in Security Operations Centers (SOCs). The author draws inspiration from Daniel Kahneman's book "Thinking, Fast and Slow," which describes two systems of human cognition: System 1 (fast, automatic, and unconscious) and System 2 (slow, logical, and effortful). The author argues that most security teams are designing their AI architecture incorrectly, using System 2 for tasks that should be automated by System 1.

The article suggests that 98% of alerts can be resolved autonomously, leaving only 2% that require human review. This mirrors Kahneman's ratio of System 1 to System 2 thinking. The author proposes a two-brain approach to SOC design, where the fast brain (System 1) handles automatic processing for the majority of inputs, and the slow brain (System 2) is reserved for deliberate human judgment on the remaining 2%. This approach would allow analysts to focus on high-priority cases, rather than being overwhelmed by manual triage of hundreds of alerts per day.

The article concludes that security teams that design their SOC architecture to mirror the human brain, with both fast and slow systems working together, will be the most successful. This approach would enable autonomous investigation of 100% of signals, with the slow brain (System 2) supervising and making deliberate judgments on escalated cases. The author also notes that bringing investigation in-house, rather than outsourcing to a Managed Detection and Response (MDR) provider, is crucial for building a knowledge layer that can be leveraged by AI copilots like Claude, Codex, and Cursor. By designing their SOC architecture correctly, security teams can make the most of their analysts' skills and improve their overall threat detection and response capabilities.
