The ransomware landscape in 2026 is characterized by the emergence of new families adopting post-quantum cryptography ciphers, making it nearly impossible for victims to decrypt their data without paying a ransom. Despite a decline in ransomware attacks, organizations across all sectors continue to face a high likelihood of attack, with the manufacturing sector alone potentially losing over $18 billion in 2025. Ransomware operators are refining their tactics, prioritizing neutralizing endpoint defenses before executing their payloads, and using tools like "EDR killers" to terminate security processes and disable monitoring agents.

The rise of encryptionless extortion attacks is also a notable trend, where attackers focus on extracting sensitive data and leveraging the threat of public disclosure as their primary means of extortion. This shift fundamentally changes the nature of the risk, as backups are no longer effective against data exposure, regulatory consequences, and reputational damage. The industrialization of initial access, with initial access brokers supplying pre-compromised access to corporate environments, has become a critical component of the ransomware ecosystem. The primary access vectors offered for sale include RDP, VPN, and RDWeb, with remote access infrastructure remaining the primary attack surface.

The dark web plays a significant role in the ransomware ecosystem, with Telegram channels and underground forums functioning as platforms for the distribution and sale of compromised datasets and access credentials. Law enforcement agencies have been actively shutting down dark web platforms and ransomware data leak sites, causing inconvenience to ransomware coordination. The top ransomware groups in 2025 included Qilin, Clop, and Akira, with Qilin standing out as one of the fastest-growing and dominant RaaS platforms. New actors in 2026, such as The Gentlemen group, are emerging with more professionalized and controlled operations, reflecting a broader shift toward data-centric extortion strategies.

The Gentlemen group is notable for its emphasis on data-centric extortion strategies, often prioritizing exfiltration and leverage over purely disruptive encryption. This aligns with one of the defining trends of 2026: ransomware evolving into a form of data breach monetization rather than just system denial. Other emerging groups, such as Devman, are also worth noting, as they likely leverage existing tools and infrastructure to launch attacks. The ransomware landscape in 2026 is characterized by increased sophistication, industrialization, and specialization, with attackers adapting to lower ransom payment rates and improved backup practices among victims.

The use of post-quantum cryptography, such as the ML-KEM standard, is becoming more prevalent, with groups like PE32 ransomware family leveraging this technology to secure their AES keys. The Kyber1024 algorithm, used by PE32, provides Level 5 security, roughly equivalent in strength to AES-256, making it nearly impossible for victims to decrypt their data without paying a ransom.
