Meta, the company behind Facebook and Instagram, has been testing face-recognition software for its smart glasses, developed by Rank One Computing, a company that sells surveillance tools to police departments and the US military. The software license, obtained by WIRED, reveals a business relationship between Meta and Rank One, with the latter's face recognition technology being considered for a mass-market consumer device. Rank One's software has been used by various law enforcement agencies, including the US Marshals Service and the Naval Criminal Investigative Service, to identify individuals.

The face-recognition system, which includes liveness detection to check if a camera is seeing a real person, was integrated into a test version of the Meta AI app that powers Meta's Ray-Ban and Oakley smart glasses. Although the system was dormant and not active for users, remnants of the integration remained in a version of the app that was shipped to millions of consumers. Meta has since deleted the face-recognition system from the app entirely. The company has declined to comment on its relationship with Rank One, citing no reason for licensing the software or whether the relationship is ongoing.

The use of face-recognition technology in consumer products raises concerns about privacy and bias, as the technology has been shown to perform differently across demographic groups. Rank One's algorithm has been found to produce false matches at varying rates depending on a person's sex and country of birth. With few national rules governing face recognition in the US, experts warn that the risks of this technology becoming a common consumer product are significant and largely unbounded. As the line between surveillance technology and consumer products continues to blur, there is a growing need for proper checks and regulations to ensure that such technology is used responsibly and with consideration for individual privacy and rights.
