Google has set a deadline of September 30, 2026, to enforce Android developer verification in four countries: Brazil, Indonesia, Singapore, and Thailand. On this date, certified Android devices in these countries will block the installation of apps from unverified developers, whether the app is downloaded from Google Play or other app stores. This means that developers who have not registered with Google will not be able to install their apps on certified devices in these countries.

The verification process requires developers to provide Google with their legal name, address, and contact details, as well as a government ID. Developers must also prove ownership of their apps by submitting a signed APK. Google has already opened registration to all developers and claims that nearly all installs on Google Play and a large majority of those from outside it are already covered. The company is also introducing APIs for bulk registration and package-name checks to make the process easier for developers.

The introduction of Android developer verification has been met with resistance from the open-source community, with some arguing that it will stifle innovation and limit the ability of independent developers to distribute their apps. F-Droid, a free-software app repository, has stated that the requirement would effectively end its project, as it relies on pseudonymous contributors who will not provide Google with their legal identity. Google has made some concessions, such as introducing an advanced flow for installing unverified apps and offering limited-distribution accounts for students and hobbyists, but the issue remains a point of contention ahead of the global rollout in 2027.
