Here is a summary of the article in three concise paragraphs:

The fastest path to AI adoption in organizations runs through security, with security leaders who build fast and visible paths to AI adoption becoming the most valued partners. According to McKinsey's State of AI report, 76% of employees now use AI in some capacity at work, often without security review. The conventional response of restriction is ineffective, as employees will find workarounds if the official approval path is too slow. Instead, security teams should focus on governance that enables employees to use AI tools safely and efficiently.

Effective AI governance involves giving employees a clear and fast path to access approved tools, request new ones, and understand why guidelines exist. This approach earns security teams a reputation for being able to move fast and add value, leading to a seat at the strategy table. A current inventory of AI tools, OAuth audits, and browser-native monitoring are essential for building a clear picture of AI usage. An effective AI acceptable use policy should list approved tools, define data categories that stay out of AI tools, confirm training opt-out status, and provide a process for requesting new tools with a turnaround time.

Security teams that approach governance as a design problem, focusing on making the secure path the one employees want to use, are the ones who earn a seat at the strategy table. By publishing the approved list, setting a turnaround time, and keeping it, organizations can see shadow AI usage decline. Employees who understand the reasoning behind AI governance are more likely to apply it as a habit, rather than just following rules. By building from this understanding, security teams can create a system that employees use willingly, and the organization starts to see security as a team that understands both people and risk.
