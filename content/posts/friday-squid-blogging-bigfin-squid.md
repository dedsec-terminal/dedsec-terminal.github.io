The article discusses the current state of cybersecurity, specifically the impact of AI and machine learning on the field. Clive Robinson expresses his concern that the over-reliance on AI and machine learning systems will lead to a decline in human skills and expertise, making it difficult for the West to defend against cyber threats. He argues that current AI systems, including large language models (LLMs), are limited to pattern matching and do not truly reason or think creatively. Robinson believes that the use of AI and machine learning systems will ultimately lead to a loss of technological capability in the West, making it vulnerable to cyber attacks.

Robinson's comments are in response to a blog post about the decline of the Capture the Flag (CTF) scene, a community of cybersecurity enthusiasts who participate in competitions to test their skills. The author of the post, Kabir, argues that the CTF scene is dying due to a lack of interest and participation from younger generations. Robinson agrees with Kabir's assessment, but attributes the decline to the over-reliance on AI and machine learning systems, which he believes are stifling human innovation and creativity.

Other commenters, including K.S. and bye bye ai, disagree with Robinson's assessment of AI and machine learning systems. They argue that LLMs are capable of reasoning and inference, and that they can be used to augment human capabilities. However, Robinson remains skeptical, citing the limitations of current AI systems and the need for human expertise and creativity in cybersecurity. He believes that the West's reliance on AI and machine learning systems will ultimately lead to a decline in its technological capabilities, making it vulnerable to cyber threats.

The discussion highlights the ongoing debate about the role of AI and machine learning in cybersecurity, and the need for human expertise and creativity in the field. While some argue that AI and machine learning systems can augment human capabilities, others believe that they are limited and may ultimately lead to a decline in human skills and expertise. The conversation underscores the importance of striking a balance between the use of technology and the development of human skills and expertise in cybersecurity.

The article also touches on the idea that the West's economic activity is increasingly based on non-tangible technology companies that earn income through social media, internet advertising, and surveillance. This has led to a decline in manufacturing and technological capabilities, making the West vulnerable to cyber threats. The discussion highlights the need for a balanced approach to technological development, one that combines the use of AI and machine learning systems with the development of human skills and expertise.
