Here are three concise paragraphs summarizing the article:

The adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in Security Operations Centers (SOCs) has grown significantly, with billions of dollars invested in AI-powered security operations platforms. However, despite this rapid adoption, only 10% of SOCs report receiving excellent value from AI, while 71% report receiving some or no value. This discrepancy suggests a structural issue, with SOCs over-investing in AI without the operational maturity to extract value from their investments.

The SOC-CMM 2026 Maturity Report highlights three key findings: AI adoption is up across all categories, with off-the-shelf AI models being the most commonly deployed; the dominant adoption pattern is the "taker model," where AI is deployed without customization; and the two biggest SOC improvement challenges are lack of best practices and complexity of increasing maturity. The report also notes that the first wave of AI SOC tools underperformed because they were designed as features bolted onto existing security products, rather than being integrated into a cohesive architecture.

To deliver excellent value, the second wave of AI in SOCs must focus on architecture, not features. This means connecting the five stages of the SOC (threat intelligence, threat hunting, detection, investigation, and remediation) into a single, agentic fabric grounded in the customer's environment. The platform should sit on top of existing security stacks, providing a connective layer that enables each stage to feed the next. With this architecture, SOCs can report sharper investigations, faster detections, and more effective remediation, ultimately moving from "some value" to "excellent value" in their AI investments.
