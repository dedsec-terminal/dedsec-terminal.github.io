The concept of protecting privacy in the era of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has been addressed by Daniel Solove in a recent Wall Street Journal article. Solove argues that simply giving individuals control over their personal data is not an effective way to regulate privacy. Instead, he suggests that companies should be held accountable for their actions, similar to the regulations in place for food and drug companies.

Solove proposes several measures to achieve this, including rigorous data minimization, fiduciary duties, and liability for negligent or reckless technological design. He also suggests holding companies liable for algorithms that cause harm and implementing multi-stakeholder reviews of technologies. These measures aim to ensure that companies prioritize privacy and take responsibility for their actions, rather than relying solely on individual control over personal data.

The idea that individual control over personal data is insufficient for protecting privacy is not surprising, as many have long argued that this approach is inadequate. The comment from "entronid" reflects this sentiment, stating that the idea should not come as a surprise to anyone. The proposed measures aim to shift the focus from individual responsibility to corporate accountability, recognizing that companies have a significant role to play in protecting privacy in the AI era.
