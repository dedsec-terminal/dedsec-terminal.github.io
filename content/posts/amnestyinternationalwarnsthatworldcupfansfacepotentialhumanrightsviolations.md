Amnesty International has warned that fans attending the 2026 FIFA World Cup in the United States, Mexico, and Canada may face human rights violations. The organization's report, "Humanity Must Win: Defending Rights, Tackling Repression at the 2026 FIFA World Cup," outlines risks to fans, players, locals, and media, including restrictions on freedom of expression and peaceful assembly. The US, where most of the matches will be played, is highlighted as a particular concern due to racial profiling and mass detentions by immigration agencies.

In the US, Amnesty International has identified a "human rights emergency" characterized by violent arrests and mass detention. The role of Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) in World Cup security has raised concerns, with the agency offering its personnel to local police departments to help with security during matches. In Mexico, the deployment of 100,000 security agents, including army members, increases the risk of repression for peaceful protesters, including a movement of searching mothers demanding justice for the country's 133,500 registered disappearances.

The report also notes concerns in Canada, where the housing crisis may lead to the displacement of unhoused populations in host cities like Toronto. Amnesty International has recommended that host cities avoid using military forces in civilian security tasks and ensure that World Cup events and venues are not subject to immigration raids. The organization has criticized the lack of planning for human rights protection, with only four of the 16 host cities having published plans at the time of the report's release in March.
