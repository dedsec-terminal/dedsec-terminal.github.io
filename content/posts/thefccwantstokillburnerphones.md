The Federal Communications Commission (FCC) has proposed a new rule that would require cellular providers to collect personal information from customers, including their name, physical address, government-issued ID number, and an alternate phone number. This move has raised concerns among privacy advocates, who argue that it would threaten the anonymity of individuals who use burner phones or private phone carriers. The proposal is intended to prevent scammers from exploiting phone networks, but critics say it would also make it more difficult for people to maintain their privacy.

The FCC's proposal has sparked concerns among journalists, whistleblowers, and activists who rely on anonymous phone services to protect their identities. A new phone carrier, Phreeli, which allows users to register with just a ZIP code, would be affected by the proposed rule. The company's founder argues that people have a right to privacy and anonymity, and that the proposed rule would undermine this right. The FCC is accepting comments on the proposal until June 25, and privacy advocates are urging people to speak out against the rule.

In other news, a cybercriminal group known as ShinyHunters has been exploiting a zero-day bug in Oracle's PeopleSoft software to breach over 100 organizations, mostly in the education sector. Google has warned about the vulnerability, and Oracle has alerted its customers. Meanwhile, Microsoft has released its largest-ever Patch Tuesday update, with over 200 bug fixes, thanks to the help of AI-enabled bug hunting. Google has also sued an alleged Chinese scam group for using its Gemini AI tool to scam hundreds of thousands of Americans. Additionally, President Trump has dropped his bid to appoint Bill Pulte as intelligence chief, following widespread criticism, and has instead nominated Jay Clayton for the role.
