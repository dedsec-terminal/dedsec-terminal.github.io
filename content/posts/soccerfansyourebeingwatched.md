The 2026 FIFA World Cup, expected to draw over 5 million fans across 16 venues in the US, Canada, and Mexico, has raised concerns about the deployment of invasive surveillance technologies. Experts warn that the Trump administration may use heightened terrorism concerns to justify the use of these technologies without adequate safeguards. The US Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) may also carry out aggressive immigration enforcement during the tournament, prompting Human Rights Watch to urge FIFA to seek an "ICE truce" for the duration of the event.

The list of surveillance technologies to be deployed at the tournament is extensive, including drone and counter-drone systems, face recognition, and AI-powered surveillance. Companies such as Fortem Technologies, Sentrycs, and Axon have secured contracts to provide these technologies, while the US Department of Homeland Security (DHS) has launched a new office dedicated to procuring and deploying drone and counter-drone technologies. The use of these technologies has raised concerns about transparency, data collection, and the potential for abuse. Experts stress the importance of knowing what types of tools will be used and what type of data will be collected, as well as how long it will be retained and how it will be used.

The deployment of surveillance technologies during the World Cup has broader implications, as it reflects a global trend of using major sporting events to normalize biometric surveillance in everyday life. The use of facial recognition technology, in particular, has raised concerns about mistakes and bias. The permanence of supposedly temporary security measures is also a concern, as surveillance infrastructure can outlast the tournament and be used to chill civil liberties and suppress human rights in the future. Experts warn that the normalization of these technologies can have lasting effects, dissolving the boundary between defense infrastructure and ordinary public policing, and potentially leading to the erosion of civil liberties and human rights.
