After a 3-month internet blackout, Iran's internet has started to return, albeit at very low levels. The government-imposed blackout, which began on February 28, had left over 90 million citizens without internet access for the majority of 2026. The reconnection appears to have been ordered by officials in Iran's government, but it is unclear if it will be temporary or permanent. Researchers have noted that the level of access is far below the country's typical baseline of global internet connectivity.

The partial restoration of connectivity was documented by internet monitoring experts at Kentik, NetBlocks, and Cloudflare, who began tracking the changes on Tuesday. Some fixed-line providers, such as the Telecommunication Company of Iran's fiber-optic service, have appeared to be restoring their services, while mobile networks have shown little change. Cybersecurity experts caution that it is too early to say exactly what will happen, as the situation is still evolving. The reconnection comes as the US government continues to negotiate with Iran about a permanent end to the war.

The Iranian regime has a history of controlling connectivity and censoring content, and it is unclear how the current situation will play out. The Supreme National Security Council had ordered the internet shutdown, but a different group formed by the president has reportedly ordered connectivity restoration. The move has drawn a legal challenge, and experts predict that continued authoritarian rule in Iran will mean more impact on the country's digital freedoms. Some warn that the country may never fully reconnect globally, and the situation may hinge heavily on negotiations between Iran and the United States.
