A Florida man, Robert Dillon, was wrongfully arrested for attempting to lure a child after a face-recognition system, FACES, matched his face to a photo of a suspect with a 93% accuracy. However, Dillon lived over 300 miles away from the scene and claimed to have never been to the city where the crime took place. The arrest was based solely on the face-recognition match, despite other evidence that pointed away from Dillon, such as the fact that the suspect was a "regular customer" at the McDonald's where the incident occurred.

The American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) has filed a lawsuit on behalf of Dillon, alleging that the police relied too heavily on the face-recognition technology and failed to conduct a proper investigation. The lawsuit names the investigating officer, the Jacksonville Sheriff's Office, and the Pinellas County Sheriff's Office, and seeks compensatory and punitive damages. The ACLU is also calling for an overhaul of the face-recognition policies used by these agencies. Dillon's case is one of at least 15 known wrongful arrests in the US attributed to face-recognition technology, and highlights the need for greater oversight and safeguards to prevent such abuses.

The FACES system, which has been in operation since 2001, has been criticized for its lack of oversight and transparency. A 2016 study found that the system was not audited for misuse, and that no reasonable suspicion was required to run a query. The ACLU is calling for police departments to adopt safeguards to prevent future wrongful arrests, such as requiring human review of face-recognition matches and implementing policies to prevent the misuse of the technology. The case has sparked concerns about the reliability of face-recognition technology and the need for greater accountability and transparency in its use by law enforcement agencies.
