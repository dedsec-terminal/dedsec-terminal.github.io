Researchers at SentinelOne have made a breakthrough in deciphering a 21-year-old piece of malware known as Fast16, which was first revealed in an NSA leak in 2017. The malware is capable of tampering with research and engineering software to undetectably sow mayhem, and it is believed to have been created by either the US government or one of its allies. Fast16 works by automatically spreading across networks and then silently manipulating computation processes in certain software applications, altering the results of those programs to cause failures that range from faulty research results to catastrophic damage to real-world equipment.

The researchers, Vitaly Kamluk and Juan Andrés Guerrero-Saade, have determined that Fast16 was designed to target specific software applications, including physical simulation software used in modeling complex systems. They found three potential types of software that the malware might have been designed to tamper with, including LS-DYNA, a software used in modeling physics problems related to nuclear weapons research. This suggests that Fast16 might have been used to subvert Iran's attempt to gain nuclear weapons, perhaps even years before the Stuxnet malware was deployed to achieve the same result.

The discovery of Fast16 rewrites the history of state-sponsored hacking, as it shows that deceptive sabotage operations have been part of the cyber playbook from much earlier than previously thought. The malware's sophistication and complexity place it in the same class as Stuxnet, and it is believed to have been designed as a long-term, subtle sabotage that would be difficult to notice. The researchers point to evidence that Fast16 might have been used to target Iran's nuclear program, including the use of LS-DYNA software by Iranian scientists carrying out research that could contribute to the development of a nuclear weapon.

The analysis of Fast16 also suggests that the malware could have been used more than once against different targets, even in different countries. The malware's code includes evidence of a version control system, along with clues that the sample analyzed wasn't the first or only version of the tool. This raises the possibility that Fast16 might have been used against other targets, including North Korea's nuclear weapons development program, which experienced numerous unexplained failures in the same time period.

The discovery of Fast16 highlights the importance of understanding the history of state-sponsored hacking and the evolution of cyber sabotage techniques.
