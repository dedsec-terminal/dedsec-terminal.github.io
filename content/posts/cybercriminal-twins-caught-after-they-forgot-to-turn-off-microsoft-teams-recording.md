Cybercriminal twins, Muneeb and Sohaib Akhter, have pleaded guilty to charges of destroying 96 government databases after being fired from their jobs at federal contractor Opexus. The brothers' revenge campaign was recorded by a Microsoft Teams meeting that they had failed to close, which was later transcribed in a court document. The recording revealed the detailed planning and execution of their attack, with Sohaib asking his brother if they were still connected to the VPN and if they should delete all the databases. The twins' mistake has hopefully driven home the lesson to future criminal hackers to close out meetings before discussing illicit activities.

In other news, Instructure, the company behind educational software Canvas, has reached a deal with the ransomware gang that hacked into its system, disrupting Canvas across thousands of US schools. The company claims that the stolen data, including records of 275 million students, has been returned and destroyed by the hackers. However, it is unclear whether Instructure paid a ransom or how much it paid. The incident highlights the thriving ecosystem of ransomware attacks and the need for companies to prioritize cybersecurity.

The alleged administrator of the dark web market Dream Market, Owe Martin Andresen, has been arrested in Germany, more than seven years after the marketplace shut down. Andresen is accused of making millions of dollars from commissions and laundering the money through gold bars. The arrest may bring to a close the longest-running dark web drug investigation of all time. Additionally, OpenAI has disclosed that two of its employees were victims of an open source hijacking attack on the TanStack library, which is used to build web apps. The company has investigated the incident and is requiring all macOS users to update their OpenAI apps by June 12.

Data broker Findem has removed code that hid its opt-out page from Google search for three years, following an investigation by Senator Maggie Hassan. The company claimed that a former employee had embedded the "no index" code without the knowledge of executives. During the time the page was de-indexed, only 679 people visited it. The incident highlights the need for transparency and accountability in the data brokering industry. Furthermore, Foxconn, the electronics manufacturing giant, has revealed that it suffered a cyberattack, with a ransomware group claiming responsibility for stealing 8 TB of data. The incident underscores the importance of cybersecurity in the manufacturing industry.
