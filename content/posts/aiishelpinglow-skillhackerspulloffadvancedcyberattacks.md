Anthropic, a company that utilizes AI systems, has published an analysis of cyber-related misuse of its AI systems. The study examined 832 accounts that were banned for malicious cyber activity between March 2025 and March 2026. The researchers mapped the observed behavior to the MITRE ATT&CK framework, which documents tactics and techniques used by attackers. They found that the most common AI-enabled activities involved preparing for cyberattacks, such as malware development, and that AI was also used to support more advanced operations like lateral movement inside compromised networks.

The study revealed a shift in how AI was being used, with AI-assisted account discovery increasing by 8.9% and AI-assisted phishing declining by 8.6%. The researchers noted that AI can now perform complex activities on behalf of less sophisticated actors, making it easier for low-skill hackers to pull off advanced cyberattacks. The analysis also showed that the number of threat actors using AI for cyber operations is increasing, with medium- and high-risk actors accounting for 56% of reviewed cases during the second half of the study period.

The study's conclusions highlight the growing role of AI in cyber operations and the need for the MITRE ATT&CK framework to evolve to capture new behaviors associated with high-risk actors. The researchers found that the highest-risk actors are distinguished by the techniques they use, and that the code, architecture, and tooling built around AI models will become a stronger indicator of risk than individual prompts. They also noted that some behaviors, such as autonomous killchain orchestration and AI-directed execution without human intervention, remain outside the framework and need to be addressed to stay ahead of emerging threats.
