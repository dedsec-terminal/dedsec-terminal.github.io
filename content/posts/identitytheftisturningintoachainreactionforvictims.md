Identity theft is becoming a complex and ongoing issue for many victims, with over 25% of those who contacted the Identity Theft Resource Center experiencing multiple identity-related incidents. According to the 2026 Trends in Identity Report, which analyzed data from 6,188 individuals, a single compromise can trigger a chain reaction that spreads across multiple accounts and institutions, making it harder for people to recover. The most common combinations of incidents reported were account takeover and unauthorized device access, as well as account takeover and new account fraud.

Lower-income victims are disproportionately affected by identity theft, with 37% experiencing multiple incidents compared to 27-33% among other income groups. These individuals are more likely to experience new account fraud, fraudulent employment, and crimes committed using their personal information, which can remain undetected for extended periods. The Identity Theft Resource Center notes that the current system is least equipped to detect and address these types of crimes, which can have serious consequences such as denied government benefits, collection notices, and problems with employment and background checks.

Child identity theft is also a significant concern, with fraudulent employment accounting for 40% of identity misuse cases involving minors. This type of theft can go undetected for years, as parents and guardians often do not monitor their child's credit or employment records. The alleged thief is often unknown, but when identified, it is often a former spouse, partner, or parent who has used the child's personal information to open accounts or obtain employment. Financial losses are also linked to poorer recovery outcomes, with victims who experienced financial harm being less likely to have their case resolved, and those with three or more financial consequences having a zero resolution rate.
