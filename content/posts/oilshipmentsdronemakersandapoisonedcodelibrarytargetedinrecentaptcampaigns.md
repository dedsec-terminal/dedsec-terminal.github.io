Here are 3 concise paragraphs summarizing the recent APT campaigns:

Between October 2025 and March 2026, state-sponsored cyber activity was driven by geopolitical pressure, with espionage groups from China, North Korea, Russia, and Iran adjusting their targets to match their governments' economic and security concerns. China-aligned groups were the most active, targeting governmental organizations, strategic industries, and advanced technology sectors in Asia. In the Middle East, Iran-aligned groups focused on Israel, targeting organizations and device manufacturers with espionage intrusions and destructive tooling.

China-aligned groups, such as FamousSparrow and SteppeDriver, targeted entities involved in oil shipments, strategic technology, and reconstruction efforts. North Korea's Lazarus umbrella compromised a widely used JavaScript library, axios, on the npm registry, and also targeted South Korean companies involved in liquid hydrogen handling and the nuclear industry. Russia-aligned groups, such as Sednit and Sandworm, continued to focus on Ukraine, deploying wiper malware and targeting military personnel and drone manufacturers. Russia also targeted a Polish energy company, highlighting the risk of cyber attacks on critical infrastructure in NATO member states.

Iran-aligned activity shifted to proxy and unattributed groups, with a drop in activity from established APT groups due to internet restrictions imposed by the Iranian regime. Pro-Iranian proxy groups targeted Israel and the United States, while unattributed clusters with Iranian characteristics, such as Rusty Boots and MoKhargosh, used wipers and backdoors to compromise systems. Other notable activity included a browser-in-the-browser phishing attack against a Japanese think tank, Android spyware aimed at Arabic-speaking users, and the compromise of a UAE defense company through a CRM server. These campaigns highlight the ongoing threat of state-sponsored cyber activity and the need for vigilance and defense against these threats.
