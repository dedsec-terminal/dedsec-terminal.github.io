Google has introduced a new feature on Android phones to combat phone scams by verifying the authenticity of callers. The feature, built into the Google Dialer, uses the RCS communication standard to digitally bind a phone number with the actual smartphone handset. When an Android user calls another Android user, their device sends a silent background confirmation signal to verify the legitimacy of the call. If the signal is missing, the Google Dialer flags the call as a potential scam, displaying a pop-up warning that the caller may not be who they claim to be.

The feature is designed to protect users from impersonation scams, which have become increasingly sophisticated with the use of AI voice-cloning tools. These scams can convincingly mimic the voice of a trusted contact or family member, making it difficult for victims to distinguish between legitimate and fake calls. The new feature aims to provide a higher level of confidence in verifying the authenticity of callers, reducing the risk of falling victim to these types of scams. By removing the contact photo from the call screen and changing the entry in the recent call log to "Unknown caller," the feature underscores the seriousness of the situation.

The feature will start rolling out in updates for all Android phones running Android 12 and later, and Google hopes it will have a significant impact in protecting users from devastating scams. While the feature is currently limited to Android devices, Google has built it on the RCS standard to ensure maximum interoperability with other platforms, including Apple's iOS. The company hopes that other manufacturers, including Apple, will adopt similar features to provide a more comprehensive solution to the problem of phone scams. With the feature, Google aims to provide an additional layer of protection for users, making it more difficult for scammers to succeed and reducing the risk of financial and emotional harm.
