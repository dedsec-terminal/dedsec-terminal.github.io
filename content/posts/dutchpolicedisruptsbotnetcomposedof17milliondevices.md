The Dutch National Police and the National Cyber Security Center (NCSC) have disrupted a massive botnet consisting of 17 million infected devices, including computers, mobile phones, IoT devices, and routers. The botnet was controlled by 200 servers located in the Netherlands, which were seized by the cybercrime unit of the Hague police. The servers were taken offline by the hosting provider due to their use for criminal purposes.

The botnet is believed to be part of the infrastructure underpinning Asocks, a commercial residential and mobile proxy service. Residential proxies use real, trusted IP addresses, making it difficult to detect or block malicious activity. The NCSC warned that residential proxies can be used for various malicious purposes, including DDoS attacks, spamming, and malware distribution. Many users may unknowingly install proxy software or have their devices compromised with malware that installs a proxy function.

The disruption of the botnet is a significant blow to cybercrime operations, which have increasingly relied on residential proxy networks to maintain anonymity and circumvent geographical restrictions. Law enforcement agencies have been cracking down on these services, with previous disruptions including 5socks, Anyproxy, and SocksEscort. The opaque nature of the residential proxy market makes it challenging to ensure that proxies are used for legal purposes, highlighting the need for continued efforts to combat cybercrime and protect against malicious activity.
