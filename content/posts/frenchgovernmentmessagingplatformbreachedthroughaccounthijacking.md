The French government's secure messaging platform, Tchap, has been breached through account hijacking. The platform, built on the open-source Matrix protocol, is used by civil servants, ministries, and public agencies for government communications. The breach was detected on June 7 by France's national cybersecurity agency, ANSSI, and an investigation has been launched to determine the extent of the compromise.

According to the Interministerial Directorate for Digital Affairs (DINUM), the incident was limited in scope and did not affect private conversations protected by end-to-end encryption. The compromised account was identified and blocked, and officials stated that any accessible information would have been limited to public chat rooms, which are open to all Tchap users and whose messages are not encrypted. However, an alleged hacker has claimed to have obtained a large amount of data, including user accounts, messages, and media files.

The claims made by the alleged hacker have not been independently verified, and it remains unclear how much data was actually accessed. DINUM has notified France's data protection authority, the CNIL, due to the potential exposure of personal data, and has alerted Tchap users that public chat rooms are not encrypted and can be accessed by any user. The investigation is ongoing, and officials are working to contain the incident and prevent future breaches.
