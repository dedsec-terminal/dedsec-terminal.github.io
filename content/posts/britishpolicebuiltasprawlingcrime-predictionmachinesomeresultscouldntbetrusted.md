The Avon and Somerset Police in the UK have been using a sprawling crime-prediction machine, known as the Think Family Database, to store sensitive information on nearly half a million people in the city of Bristol. The database, launched in 2016, uses machine-learning models to assign risk scores to thousands of adults and children, aiming to identify potential threats and vulnerabilities. However, the program has been shrouded in secrecy, with many residents unaware of its existence or how their data is being used.

An investigation by WIRED, in partnership with other news organizations, has revealed that at least two of the risk-scoring models were abandoned due to concerns over their reliability. Documents show that government inspectors and independent reviewers have raised concerns about the lack of transparency and potential biases in the systems. The investigation also found that some of the predictive models had "genuinely poor predictive performance," according to an independent analyst. Despite these concerns, the UK appears to be embracing predictive analytics and artificial intelligence in its criminal justice system, with the former chief constable of Avon and Somerset Police now leading the national standard-setting body for police forces.

The development of the Think Family Database was led by a team of council and police staff, who pulled together data from various public agencies to identify children and families at risk. However, the team did not seek residents' consent to use their data, relying instead on "legal gateways" that allowed data sharing to protect children. The project's leaders believed it helped protect children, but the lack of transparency and potential biases in the systems have raised concerns about the program's impact on public trust. As the UK moves to expand the use of predictive analytics in policing, concerns about accountability, transparency, and bias will need to be addressed to ensure that these systems are used effectively and fairly.
