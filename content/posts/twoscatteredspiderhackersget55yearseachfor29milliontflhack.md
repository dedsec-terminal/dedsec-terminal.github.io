Two hackers, Owen Flowers (18) and Thalha Jubair (20), have been sentenced to 5.5 years each for their role in a 2024 hack of Transport for London (TfL) that caused £29 million in losses and recovery costs. The hack, which occurred between August 31 and September 3, 2024, left 148 TfL systems inoperable and forced all 27,000 employees to reset their passwords in person. The attack also affected various services, including Dial-a-Ride, digital payments, and concessionary travel cards.

The hackers, who pleaded guilty to the charges, are believed to be members of the Scattered Spider extortion crew, which is accused of carrying out hundreds of attacks between 2022 and 2025. The group is also known for its involvement in data extortion, SIM swapping, and social engineering. Flowers and Jubair are the first hackers to be successfully prosecuted under Section 3ZA of the Computer Misuse Act 1990, which is the most serious charge under the Act. The case is considered the biggest cybercrime prosecution in the UK courts.

The investigation and prosecution of the case involved international cooperation, with the National Crime Agency (NCA) and the Crown Prosecution Service (CPS) working together to gather evidence and build a case against the hackers. The NCA has stated that the action against Flowers and Jubair has effectively halted the Scattered Spider group, but notes that other criminals may continue to use the brand. The case highlights the importance of early reporting of cybercrime to law enforcement and the need for individuals and organizations to take steps to protect themselves from cyber threats.
