The INC ransomware group has emerged as a major threat in 2026, with over 830 victims since August 2023. The group's rise to prominence can be attributed to the disruption of other ransomware operations, such as LockBit and BlackCat, which led to affiliates migrating to alternative operations like INC. The majority of INC's victims are based in the United States, with targeted sectors including legal services, manufacturing, construction, technology, and healthcare.

INC's ransomware operation has undergone significant developments, including the rewriting of its Windows and Linux/ESXi encryptors in Rust to facilitate easier cross-platform development and better resist reverse engineering efforts. The group's attacks often involve the use of an updated credential dumper that can target newer Veeam backup deployments. Additionally, the sale of INC's Windows and Linux variants on the cybercrime underground has led to the emergence of related ransomware families, such as Lynx and Sinobi, with significant code overlap.

The INC ransomware group's attack chain typically involves obtaining initial access through various methods, including spear-phishing and exploiting vulnerabilities in public-facing applications. The group then extracts sensitive credentials, uses living-off-the-land binaries for lateral movement, and employs techniques like the bring your own vulnerable drive (BYOVD) to impair system defenses. The group's use of widely known techniques, rather than advanced tradecraft or bespoke tooling, has allowed it to scale up and produce a steady stream of victims across various geographies and sectors. As a result, INC has become one of the most prolific cybercrime groups, with over 120 incidents reported in Q1 2026.
