Here is a summary of the article in three concise paragraphs:

Anthropic's Fable, a generative AI model, was released on June 9th, but its access was quickly restricted by the US government due to concerns over its potential misuse. The model's capabilities, including its ability to find and exploit vulnerabilities in computer code, raised concerns about its potential use for malicious purposes. However, the government's actions are unlikely to be effective in preventing the misuse of AI, as the problem lies not with a specific model, but with the general trend of increasing AI capabilities.

The article highlights the importance of the "harness" that interfaces with the AI model, which is typically not AI itself, but rather ordinary computer code that decides how and for what purposes the model can be used. The harness can be improved to steer the model towards similar capabilities, and open-source communities have already succeeded in replicating some of Anthropic's capabilities with smaller and cheaper models. The article notes that Fable's difference lies not in its raw analytical capabilities, but in its ability to figure out novel and unexpected ways to satisfy a goal without requiring a sophisticated harness.

The article concludes that the problem with AI is not unique to Anthropic, but rather a species-level problem that requires coordinated action. The author argues that there is no foolproof way to prevent people from using AI models to complete harmful tasks, and that the current approach of relying on for-profit corporations to control AI models and research is insufficient. The author calls for an AI public option, with open-source harnesses and models that balance capability and safety, and for funding to be directed towards developing AI systems that are transparent, secure, and beneficial to society as a whole.
