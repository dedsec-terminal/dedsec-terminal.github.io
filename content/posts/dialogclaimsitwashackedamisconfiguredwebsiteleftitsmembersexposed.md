Dialog, an invite-only group cofounded by Peter Thiel, notified its members and past event participants that their personal information had been breached due to a supposed hack by a criminal hacker. However, an analysis by WIRED found that the data was actually exposed due to a misconfigured website, making it publicly accessible to anyone who visited a landing page for the group's app. The exposed data included the names of 113 past participants, as well as information on people registered for an upcoming retreat, including senior figures in national security and technology.

The misconfiguration allowed anyone to sign up for the app using any email address, without a password, and access internal files containing sensitive information, including private contact details, dates of birth, and emergency contacts. The data also included participant lists, schedules, and links to completed questionnaires, which contained even more sensitive information. Cybersecurity experts described the incident as a result of negligence and a common web design error, rather than a sophisticated intrusion. The incident has raised concerns about the group's handling of sensitive information and its attempts to characterize the exposure as a "cyberattack" by a "known cybercriminal".

The exposure has also sparked a public reaction from prominent attendees, who have sought to explain their presence on the list. Some, such as Ezra Klein and Joseph Gordon-Levitt, have downplayed their involvement with the group, while others, like Sophia Bush, have expressed surprise and discomfort at the group's cofounder, Peter Thiel. The incident has also raised questions about the group's secrecy and its attempts to silence journalists and researchers, with some warning that broad computer-crime laws are being used to chill security research and journalism. Dialog has demanded that WIRED hand over a copy of the data, but the publication has refused, citing its right to protect its sources and reporting.
