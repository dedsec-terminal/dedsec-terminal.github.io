A recent leak of San Francisco Police Department (SFPD) drone footage has exposed the extent of urban surveillance in the city. The leak, which was accidentally livestreamed on Skydio's website, showed real-time footage from five surveillance drones, including color and thermal imaging, location metadata, and drone pilots' names and email addresses. The footage, which was discovered by two security researchers, Sam Curry and Maik Robert, included scenes of police chases, arrests, and searches, as well as surveillance of individuals and vehicles.

The leaked footage, which was captured over a 48-hour period, included 60 videos from 20 separate flights, showing hundreds of people and vehicles. The videos also included clear faces of dozens of people, raising concerns about privacy. The SFPD has stated that the link to the drone footage was an "internal restricted link" that was "improperly obtained and accessed" by the researchers, but Curry and Robert argue that they did not bypass any security or authentication to access the stream. The SFPD has since put in place more restrictive sharing protocols to prevent similar leaks in the future.

The leak has raised questions about the necessity and proportionality of the surveillance, with many of the videos showing innocuous scenes, such as people playing basketball or sitting on a roof. The SFPD has stated that it adheres to a "strict policy" around drone use, but the leak has highlighted the potential for abuse and the need for greater transparency and oversight. The incident has also sparked concerns about the growing use of drones by law enforcement agencies, with Skydio being one of the leading companies selling drones to police departments and government agencies. The leak has sparked a wider debate about the balance between public safety and individual privacy in the age of urban surveillance.
